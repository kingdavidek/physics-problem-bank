1 What is BIDMAS? All Exam Boards Year 7

When a calculation has more than one operation — like 3 + 2 × 4 — you need to know which part to work out first. Different people might get different answers if they don't follow the same rule!

BIDMAS (also called BODMAS) is the agreed order everyone follows. It stands for:

Letter Meaning Example
BBrackets(3 + 2) × 4 → brackets first → 5 × 4
IIndices (powers & roots)3² + 1 → 9 + 1
D / MDivision and Multiplication (left → right)8 ÷ 4 × 2 → 2 × 2 → 4 (not 1!)
A / SAddition and Subtraction (left → right)10 − 3 + 2 → 7 + 2 → 9 (not 5!)

Example: Work out 3 + 2 × 4

  • There are no brackets or powers, so look for × or ÷ first.
  • Multiply: 2 × 4 = 8
  • Add: 3 + 8 = 11

If you just worked left to right you'd get 3 + 2 = 5, then 5 × 4 = 20. That's wrong. BIDMAS gives 11.

⚠️ Equal priority: Division and Multiplication have the same priority — go left to right. Same for Addition and Subtraction. So 8 ÷ 4 × 2 = (8 ÷ 4) × 2 = 2 × 2 = 4, not 8 ÷ (4 × 2) = 1.

2 BIDMAS with Brackets and Powers All Exam Boards Year 8

When you have brackets and powers in the same calculation, always work through the BIDMAS steps one at a time. Label each step as you go — that way you won't lose track.

Example: Calculate 3 + 2 × (7 − 4)²

  1. Brackets: (7 − 4) = 3  →  expression becomes 3 + 2 × 3²
  2. Indices: 3² = 9  →  expression becomes 3 + 2 × 9
  3. Multiply: 2 × 9 = 18  →  expression becomes 3 + 18
  4. Add: 3 + 18 = 21

Example: Calculate (2 + 3)² ÷ 5 − 1

  1. Brackets: (2 + 3) = 5  →  expression becomes 5² ÷ 5 − 1
  2. Indices: 5² = 25  →  expression becomes 25 ÷ 5 − 1
  3. Divide: 25 ÷ 5 = 5  →  expression becomes 5 − 1
  4. Subtract: 5 − 1 = 4

Exam tip: Rewrite the expression at each step rather than trying to do everything in your head. Examiners can give method marks even if you make an arithmetic error later.

3 Negative Numbers — Adding and Subtracting All Exam Boards Year 7

Negative numbers sit to the left of zero on a number line. You can picture them as temperatures below zero or floors below ground in a building.

The key rule for adding and subtracting is: two signs next to each other combine into one sign. Think of it this way:

  • + − = −   (adding a negative = moving left = subtracting)
  • − − = +   (subtracting a negative = moving right = adding)

Examples:

  • 5 + (−3) = 5 − 3 = 2
  • 5 − (−3) = 5 + 3 = 8
  • −4 + (−2) = −4 − 2 = −6
  • −1 − (−5) = −1 + 5 = 4

Memory trick: Two minus signs next to each other = double negative = positive. Like the grammar rule "a double negative makes a positive" (e.g. "I don't have no money" means you do have money!).

4 Negative Numbers — Multiplying and Dividing All Exam Boards Year 8

Multiplying and dividing with negatives has a simple two-case rule:

+
Same signs
positive × positive = positive
negative × negative = positive
−
Different signs
positive × negative = negative
negative × positive = negative

You must know the sign rules for all four operations:

SignsResultExample
+ × ++3 × 4 = 12
− × −+(−3) × (−4) = 12
+ × −−3 × (−4) = −12
− × +−(−3) × 4 = −12

The same rules apply to division: same signs → positive, different signs → negative.

Examples with division:

  • (−12) ÷ (−3) = +4 (same signs → positive)
  • 15 ÷ (−5) = −3 (different signs → negative)
  • (−20) ÷ 4 = −5 (different signs → negative)

5 Negative Numbers with Powers All Exam Boards Year 9

This is one of the biggest trap questions in GCSE. The position of the brackets completely changes the answer.

Compare these two carefully:

(−3)²
The negative is inside the brackets.
The whole thing gets squared.
(−3) × (−3) = +9
negative × negative = positive
−(3²)
The minus is outside the brackets — only 3 is squared.
−(3 × 3) = −9
apply the negative after squaring

Be very careful with brackets when squaring negative numbers.

  • (−3)² = (−3) × (−3) = +9 — the negative is inside the brackets, so it gets squared
  • −(3²) = −(3 × 3) = −9 — only 3 is squared; the negative sign stays outside
⚠️ This is one of the most common errors in GCSE exams. Always check whether the negative is inside or outside the brackets before squaring.

6 Combining BIDMAS with Negatives All Exam Boards Year 9

Now we put it all together. Work through BIDMAS step by step, applying the sign rules at each stage. Write out every step — never try to do two things at once.

Example: Calculate −2 × (3 + 5) − (−4)²

  1. Brackets: (3 + 5) = 8 → expression becomes −2 × 8 − (−4)²
  2. Indices: (−4)² = +16 → expression becomes −2 × 8 − 16
  3. Multiply: −2 × 8 = −16 → expression becomes −16 − 16
  4. Subtract: −16 − 16 = −32

Example: Calculate (−3)² + 4 × (−2)

  1. Indices: (−3)² = 9 → expression becomes 9 + 4 × (−2)
  2. Multiply: 4 × (−2) = −8 → expression becomes 9 + (−8)
  3. Add: 9 + (−8) = 9 − 8 = 1

Strategy: At each step, ask yourself: "Which operation comes next in BIDMAS?" Handle one operation at a time and rewrite the expression after each step. This keeps things clear and reduces errors.

7 BIDMAS & Negative Numbers — Quick Reference All Exam Boards Year 9 Revision Card

BIDMAS & Negative Numbers — Quick Reference

BIDMAS order

  • B — Brackets first
  • I — Indices (powers & roots)
  • D / M — Divide & multiply, left to right
  • A / S — Add & subtract, left to right

Negative numbers

  • \(a + (-b) = a - b\)
  • \(a - (-b) = a + b\)
  • Same signs × or ÷ → positive
  • Different signs × or ÷ → negative
  • \((-n)^2\) is positive; \(-(n^2)\) is negative

Common exam traps

  • \(8 \div 4 \times 2 = 4\), not 1 — work D and M left to right
  • \(10 - 3 + 2 = 9\), not 5 — work A and S left to right
  • \((-3)^2 = +9\) but \(-(3^2) = -9\) — brackets change everything
