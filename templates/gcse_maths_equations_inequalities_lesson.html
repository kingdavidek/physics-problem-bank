Equations and Inequalities

In this topic we find unknown numbers in equations and inequalities. You will use the same “balance” idea throughout: whatever you do to one side, you must do to the other.

1 Solving One-Step and Two-Step Equations All Exam Boards Year 7

An equation is a maths sentence with an equals sign. The left side has the same value as the right side. Your job is to find the unknown letter — usually x.

Picture a set of scales. If we take 7 kg off the left pan, we must take 7 kg off the right pan too, or the scales tip. Equations work the same way.

One-step example: Solve x + 7 = 12

  • We want x on its own, so subtract 7 from both sides.
  • 12 − 7 = 5, so x = 5.
  • Check: 5 + 7 = 12 ✓

Two-step example: Solve 3x + 4 = 19

  • First undo the +4: subtract 4 from both sides → 3x = 15.
  • Then undo the ×3: divide both sides by 3 → x = 5.

In general, for ax + b = c, we undo the +b first, then divide by a — just like in the example above.

Always check: put your answer back in. Here, 3 × 5 + 4 = 19 ✓

🧠 Quick Check

Solve 3x + 4 = 19.

2 Equations with Brackets All Exam Boards Year 8

Sometimes x is inside brackets. We usually expand the bracket first, then solve like a normal two-step equation.

Example: Solve 2(3x − 1) = 16

  • Multiply the 2 into the bracket: 2 × 3x = 6x and 2 × (−1) = −2 → 6x − 2 = 16
  • Add 2 to both sides: 6x = 18
  • Divide both sides by 6: x = 3
  • Check: 2(3 × 3 − 1) = 2(8) = 16 ✓

Rule of thumb: expand first unless the bracket is already on its own (e.g. 3(x + 2) = 15 — you could divide by 3 straight away).

🧠 Quick Check

Solve 2(3x − 1) = 16.

3 Unknowns on Both Sides All Exam Boards Year 9

Trickier equations have x on both sides. We tidy them up by moving all the x terms to one side and all the number terms to the other.

Example: Solve 5x + 2 = 2x + 17

  • Subtract 2x from both sides (smaller x-term first is often easier): 3x + 2 = 17
  • Subtract 2 from both sides: 3x = 15
  • Divide both sides by 3: x = 5
  • Check: left = 5(5) + 2 = 27, right = 2(5) + 17 = 27 ✓

Solve 5x + 2 = 2x + 17.

4 Formulae and Rearranging Problem Solving Year 10

A formula is a ready-made equation that links quantities — like area, speed, or time. The letter you want on its own is called the subject.

Example: The area of a circle is A = πr². Make r the subject so we can find the radius from the area.

  • Divide both sides by π: A ÷ π = r²
  • Take the square root of both sides: r = √(A ÷ π)

We use the same balance idea as normal equations — just undo what is “stuck” to r (multiply, divide, square, and so on).

Formulae worth knowing: speed = distance ÷ time; v = u + at (motion); triangle area A = ½ × base × height.

🧠 Quick Check

Make r the subject of A = πr².

5 Linear Inequalities All Exam Boards Year 9

An inequality uses >, <, ≥, or ≤ instead of =. It tells you a range of values that work, not just one answer.

You solve it almost the same way as an equation — but keep the inequality symbol in every step.

Example: Solve 3x − 1 > 8

  • Add 1 to both sides: 3x > 9
  • Divide both sides by 3: x > 3 (any number bigger than 3 works)
⚠️ Watch out: If you multiply or divide by a negative number, flip the inequality sign.

Example: −2x ≤ 6 → divide both sides by −2 → x ≥ −3 (the ≤ flips to ≥).

🧠 Quick Check

Solve 3x − 1 > 8.

6 Representing Inequalities on a Number Line All Exam Boards Year 9

Once you have solved an inequality, a number line is a clear way to show the answer.

  • Open circle ○ — the end value is not included (for > or <)
  • Filled circle ● — the end value is included (for ≥ or ≤)
  • Draw a line (and an arrow if the answer goes on forever) through the values that work.

Example: −1 < x ≤ 4 means x is bigger than −1 but can be 4. On a number line: open circle at −1, filled circle at 4, line between them. Whole-number answers: 0, 1, 2, 3, 4.

🧠 Quick Check

For x > 2 on a number line, what do we draw at 2?

7 Simultaneous Equations All Exam Boards Year 10 Higher Skill

Simultaneous equations are two equations at once, usually with x and y. You need one pair of values that makes both true — where the lines cross on a graph.

Two main methods: elimination (add or subtract equations to knock out x or y) and substitution (replace one letter with an expression from the other equation).

Elimination example:

3x + 2y = 13 … (1)
x + 2y = 7 … (2)

  • Both have 2y, so subtract (2) from (1): 2x = 6 → x = 3
  • Put x = 3 into (2): 3 + 2y = 7 → 2y = 4 → y = 2
  • Solution: x = 3, y = 2

Substitution: If one equation already says y = … (or x = …), plug that straight into the other equation.

🧠 Quick Check

Solve: 3x + 2y = 13 and x + 2y = 7.

8 Quadratic Equations All Exam Boards Year 10 Higher Skill

A quadratic equation has an x² term (and no higher powers). The graph is a U-shaped curve called a parabola.

Factorising example: Solve x² − x − 6 = 0

  • We need two numbers that multiply to −6 and add to −1: those are −3 and 2.
  • So (x − 3)(x + 2) = 0 — check by expanding if you like.
  • Either bracket is zero: x = 3 or x = −2.

Other tools you will meet: the quadratic formula (works when factorising is hard) and completing the square (helpful for turning-point form).

\[x = \frac{-b \pm \sqrt{b^2 - 4ac}}{2a}\]

Use this when the equation is ax² + bx + c = 0 and factorising does not come easily.

The discriminant (b² − 4ac) tells you how many real answers you get:

  • Positive → two different answers (curve crosses the x-axis twice)
  • Zero → one repeated answer (curve just touches the x-axis)
  • Negative → no real answers (curve does not cross the x-axis)

🧠 Quick Check

Solve x² − x − 6 = 0 by factorising.

9 Changing the Subject All Exam Boards Year 10–11

Changing the subject means rewriting a formula so your chosen letter is alone on one side — e.g. make t the subject of v = u + at.

Everyday idea: A recipe says “servings = 2 × people + 1”. If you know servings and want people, you rearrange — same skill as changing the subject.

Linear example: Make x the subject of y = 3x + 5

  • Subtract 5: y − 5 = 3x
  • Divide by 3: x = (y − 5) ÷ 3

With a square: Make r the subject of A = πr² → divide by π, then square root: r = √(A ÷ π).

With a fraction: Make x the subject of y = (2x + 1) / 5 → multiply by 5 first, then undo +1 and ÷2.

Exam tip: Undo operations in reverse order — what was done last to the subject is undone first.
