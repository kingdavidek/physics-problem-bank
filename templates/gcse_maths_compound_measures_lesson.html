Compound Measures

A compound measure joins two everyday measures together — like how fast you go (speed), how heavy something is for its size (density), or how hard a force presses on an area (pressure). We use simple triangles and unit conversions to solve GCSE questions.

1 Speed, Distance and Time All Exam Boards Year 9

Example: You cycle 60 km in 2 hours. Your average speed is 60 ÷ 2 = 30 km/h.

Speed, distance and time are linked. You can picture them in the SDT triangle — cover the letter you want and the triangle tells you what to do:

Cover D:  D = S × T

Cover S:  S = D ÷ T

Cover T:  T = D ÷ S

D = Distance (km, m, miles)

S = Speed (km/h, m/s, mph)

T = Time (hours, seconds, minutes)

Rules to remember:

  • Keep your units matching. If speed is in km/h, use hours for time and km for distance.
  • To use the triangle, cover what you want. Cover the top → multiply the two bottom letters. Cover a bottom letter → the top goes above the other bottom (divide).
  • If time is in hours and minutes, turn it into decimal hours first. For example, 1 h 30 min = 1.5 h.

Average speed trap: Average speed = total distance ÷ total time, NOT the average of the two speeds. For a journey at speed s₁ then s₂ over equal distances, average = 2s₁s₂/(s₁+s₂) (the harmonic mean).

On a distance–time graph, the gradient (steepness) of each straight line is the speed. In the graph above, the first part is steeper (60 km/h) and the second part is gentler (30 km/h).

A car drives 90 km in 3 hours at a steady speed. How fast is it going?

2 Density, Mass and Volume All Exam Boards Year 10

Example: A metal block has mass 200 g and volume 100 cm³. Its density is 200 ÷ 100 = 2 g/cm³ — that means 2 grams in every 1 cm³ of space.

Density tells you how “squashed in” the mass is. The DMV triangle works just like SDT:

Cover M:  M = ρ × V

Cover ρ:  ρ = M ÷ V

Cover V:  V = M ÷ ρ

M = Mass (g, kg)

ρ = Density (g/cm³, kg/m³)

V = Volume (cm³, m³, L)

Common densities to know:

Material Density (g/cm³) Density (kg/m³)
Water1.01000
Aluminium2.72700
Iron / Steel7.87800
Copper8.98900
Lead11.311300
Gold19.319300

Floating and sinking: An object floats in a liquid if its density is less than the liquid's density. Since water has density 1 g/cm³, any object with density < 1 g/cm³ floats.

3 Pressure, Force and Area All Exam Boards Year 10

Example: A box weighs 400 N and its base covers 2 m². The pressure on the floor is 400 ÷ 2 = 200 Pa (pascals).

Pressure is how much force is pushing on each square metre (or cm²) of surface. We use the PFA triangle:

Cover F:  F = P × A

Cover P:  P = F ÷ A

Cover A:  A = F ÷ P

F = Force (N, Newtons)

P = Pressure (Pa, N/m², N/cm²)

A = Area (m², cm²)

Units of pressure:

  • Pa (Pascal) = 1 N/m²  (SI unit)
  • N/cm²: to convert to Pa, multiply by 10 000 (since 1 m² = 10 000 cm²)
  • Weight (force in N) = mass (kg) × g, where g = 10 N/kg (or 9.8 N/kg)

Hydraulic systems (Pascal's Law): Pressure is transmitted equally through a fluid. If the input force acts on a small piston (small A) and the output piston has a large area, the output force is amplified: Fout = P × Aout = (Fin/Ain) × Aout

Quick Check

A force of 600 N acts on an area of 3 m². What is the pressure?

4 Unit Conversions for Compound Measures All Exam Boards Year 10

Example: A car travels at 72 km/h. To change to m/s, divide by 3.6: 72 ÷ 3.6 = 20 m/s.

Speed conversions (km/h ↔ m/s):

m/s = km/h ÷ 3.6     km/h = m/s × 3.6

This comes from: 1 km = 1000 m and 1 hour = 3600 s, so 1 km/h = 1000/3600 m/s = 5/18 m/s.

Example: Aluminium has density 2.7 g/cm³. In kg/m³ that is 2.7 × 1000 = 2700 kg/m³.

Density conversions (g/cm³ ↔ kg/m³):

kg/m³ = g/cm³ × 1000

Reason: 1 g = 0.001 kg and 1 cm³ = 10⁻⁶ m³, so 1 g/cm³ = 0.001/10⁻⁶ = 1000 kg/m³.

Pressure conversions (N/cm² ↔ Pa):

Pa = N/cm² × 10 000     (since 1 m² = 10 000 cm²)

Convert 54 km/h to m/s.

5 Population Density and Other Rates All Exam Boards Year 10

Example: A town has 36,000 people in an area of 60 km². Population density = 36,000 ÷ 60 = 600 people per km².

The same triangle idea works whenever you have Rate = Quantity ÷ Measure:

Measure Formula Units
Population Density Population ÷ Area people/km²
Flow Rate Volume ÷ Time L/min, cm³/s, m³/h
Concentration Mass of solute ÷ Volume g/L, mol/L
Water Pressure P = ρgh Pa (ρ in kg/m³, g = 10, h in m)

If water flows at 5 L/min for 20 minutes, you get 5 × 20 = 100 litres — that is volume = rate × time.

Quick Check

A pipe delivers 8 L/min. How much water flows in 15 minutes?

6 Average Speed — Common Exam Traps All Exam Boards Year 11

Example: You drive from A to B at 40 km/h and drive back at 60 km/h. Do NOT average the speeds as (40+60)/2 = 50 km/h — that is wrong!

The most common mistake in speed questions is averaging the speeds instead of using total distance ÷ total time.

Example: Return journey at different speeds
A car goes from A to B at 40 km/h and returns at 60 km/h.

  • Wrong: average = (40+60)/2 = 50 km/h ✗
  • Correct method: let AB = d km.
    Time A→B = d/40;  Time B→A = d/60
    Total time = d/40 + d/60 = d(3+2)/120 = 5d/120 = d/24
    Average = 2d ÷ (d/24) = 48 km/h ✓
  • Formula: Average = 2s₁s₂/(s₁+s₂) = 2×40×60/100 = 48 km/h

Multi-leg journeys: Always use total distance ÷ total time. Work out each leg (D = S × T), add all the distances, then add all the times.

Meeting problems: When two people walk or drive towards each other, their relative speed is the sum of their speeds. Time to meet = gap ÷ relative speed.

Overtaking problems: When a faster object catches a slower one, the closing speed is the difference of the speeds. Time to catch = head-start distance ÷ closing speed.

Quick Check

You go to town at 30 km/h and return at 60 km/h on the same road. What is your average speed for the whole return trip?
