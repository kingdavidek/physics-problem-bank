Physics Revision Lesson: Radioactivity

This page covers the key Edexcel Combined Science radioactivity ideas: atomic structure, isotopes, alpha/beta/gamma radiation, activity, background radiation, half-life, and contamination versus irradiation.

What you need to know

  • Atoms contain protons and neutrons in the nucleus, with electrons in shells around it.
  • Isotopes are atoms of the same element with different numbers of neutrons.
  • Unstable nuclei decay randomly and emit alpha, beta, or gamma radiation.
  • Activity is measured in becquerels, and half-life tells you how quickly a source decays.
  • Contamination is usually more dangerous than irradiation if radioactive material enters the body.

Atomic structure and isotopes

All atoms have a tiny nucleus containing protons and neutrons, with electrons found outside the nucleus. The proton number tells you which element the atom is.

Isotopes are atoms of the same element with the same number of protons but different numbers of neutrons. Some isotopes are stable, while others are unstable and radioactive.

  • Proton: relative charge +1
  • Neutron: relative charge 0
  • Electron: relative charge -1
  • Mass number = protons + neutrons

Quick memory tip

  • Element = decided by proton number
  • Isotope = same protons, different neutrons
  • Nucleus = protons + neutrons only

Alpha, beta and gamma radiation

Radioactive nuclei can emit three main types of nuclear radiation. You need to compare them in terms of what they are, how ionising they are, and how penetrating they are.

Activity and count rate

Activity is the rate at which unstable nuclei decay. It is measured in becquerels (Bq).

1 Bq means 1 decay per second.

In practical work, you often measure count rate using a detector. This is not exactly the same as activity, but it is used as an experimental measure of it.

Background radiation

Background radiation is the low-level radiation always present in the environment.

  • Radon gas from rocks and soil
  • Cosmic rays from space
  • Medical sources such as X-rays
  • Radioactive materials in food and building materials

In calculations, remember to subtract the background count first.

Half-life

The half-life of a radioactive isotope is the time taken for half the unstable nuclei in a sample to decay, or for the activity / count rate to fall to half its original value.

Radioactive decay is random, so you cannot predict when one nucleus will decay. But in a large sample, the overall pattern is predictable.

  • After 1 half-life: 1/2 remains
  • After 2 half-lives: 1/4 remains
  • After 3 half-lives: 1/8 remains
  • After 4 half-lives: 1/16 remains

Half-life checklist

  • Check whether the question is about mass, activity, or count rate
  • Work out how many half-lives have passed
  • Halve the value each time
  • If count rate is measured experimentally, subtract background when needed

Irradiation vs contamination

Irradiation means exposure to radiation. The object does not become radioactive just because radiation passes through or onto it.

Contamination means radioactive material gets onto or into an object or person.

Internal contamination is especially dangerous because the source keeps irradiating tissue from inside the body.

Reducing risk

  • Spend less time near the source
  • Increase distance from the source
  • Use appropriate shielding
  • Avoid contamination by handling sources carefully

For internal risk, alpha can be very dangerous even though it is not very penetrating externally.

Uses of nuclear radiation

Radiation Example use Why it is suitable
Alpha Smoke alarms Strong ionisation makes it good at ionising air.
Beta Thickness monitoring Some passes through material, so the detector reading changes if thickness changes.
Gamma Medical tracers / sterilising equipment Highly penetrating, so it can leave the body or pass through packaging.

Exam tips

Common mistakes

  • Saying irradiation makes an object radioactive.
  • Mixing up ionising power and penetrating power.
  • Forgetting to subtract background count.
  • Confusing mass number with atomic number.
  • Using the wrong number of half-lives.

What examiners like

  • Precise definitions, especially for half-life and activity.
  • Clear comparisons of alpha, beta and gamma.
  • Explicit reference to unstable nuclei and random decay.
  • Correct use of “same protons, different neutrons” for isotopes.
  • Linking a radiation type to its properties in use questions.
