1 Pythagoras' Theorem (Review) All Exam Boards Year 9

Before SOH CAH TOA, you need Pythagoras’ theorem. It only works in right-angled triangles.

Example: A right-angled triangle has shorter sides 3 cm and 4 cm. Find the hypotenuse.

  • Square and add: 3² + 4² = 9 + 16 = 25
  • Square root: √25 = 5 cm

In general, if the shorter sides are a and b, and the hypotenuse is c:

\(a^2 + b^2 = c^2\)

c is the hypotenuse — the longest side, opposite the right angle.

You can rearrange this to find any side:

  • To find the hypotenuse: \(c = \sqrt{a^2 + b^2}\)
  • To find a shorter side: \(a = \sqrt{c^2 - b^2}\)

Common mistake: When finding a shorter side, subtract squares — do not add. The hypotenuse must always be the longest side.

2 The Three Trigonometric Ratios (SOH CAH TOA) All Exam Boards Year 10

In a right-angled triangle, we name the sides relative to an angle θ:

  • Hypotenuse (H): longest side, opposite the right angle
  • Opposite (O): side facing θ
  • Adjacent (A): side next to θ (not the hypotenuse)

Memory trick: SOH CAH TOA

SOH CAH TOA

\(\sin \theta = \dfrac{\text{Opposite}}{\text{Hypotenuse}}\)
\(\cos \theta = \dfrac{\text{Adjacent}}{\text{Hypotenuse}}\)
\(\tan \theta = \dfrac{\text{Opposite}}{\text{Adjacent}}\)

How to use them:

  1. Label H, O, A for your angle θ.
  2. Decide which two sides you know or need.
  3. Pick sin, cos, or tan to connect those sides.
  4. Substitute and solve.

Finding an angle? Use the inverse on your calculator: \(\sin^{-1}\), \(\cos^{-1}\), or \(\tan^{-1}\) (often SHIFT + sin/cos/tan).

4 Using Trigonometry to Find Missing Sides All Exam Boards Year 10

If you know one side and one angle (besides the right angle), you can find another side using SOH CAH TOA.

Example: A right-angled triangle has angle θ = 35° and hypotenuse = 8 cm. Find the opposite side.

  1. Label: H = 8, O = ?, A = not needed.
  2. Ratio connecting O and H: sin.
  3. \(\sin 35° = \dfrac{O}{8}\) → \(O = 8 \times \sin 35°\)
  4. Calculator: \(O = 8 \times 0.5736 = 4.59 \text{ cm}\).

The process is identical for cos and tan — just rearrange the formula to isolate the unknown side.

5 Using Trigonometry to Find Missing Angles All Exam Boards Year 10

If you know two sides in a right-angled triangle, you can find an angle using the inverse buttons.

Example: A right-angled triangle has O = 5 cm and A = 12 cm. Find the angle θ.

  1. Label: O = 5, A = 12.
  2. Ratio connecting O and A: tan.
  3. \(\tan \theta = \dfrac{5}{12}\) → \(\theta = \tan^{-1}\left(\dfrac{5}{12}\right)\)
  4. Calculator: \(\theta = 22.6°\).

Key reminder: The inverse buttons on your calculator are \(\sin^{-1}\), \(\cos^{-1}\) and \(\tan^{-1}\). You normally access them by pressing SHIFT or 2nd.

6 The Sine Rule Higher Tier — All Boards Year 11

The sine rule works in any triangle — not just right-angled ones. It links each side to the sine of the angle opposite it.

Example: In triangle ABC, angle A = 40°, angle B = 60°, and side a = 8 cm. Find side b.

  • We know side a and angle B (opposite sides and angles pair up)
  • \(\dfrac{8}{\sin 40°} = \dfrac{b}{\sin 60°}\)
  • \(b = \dfrac{8 \times \sin 60°}{\sin 40°} = 10.8 \text{ cm}\) (1 d.p.)

\(\dfrac{a}{\sin A} = \dfrac{b}{\sin B} = \dfrac{c}{\sin C}\)

Use the sine rule when you know:

  • Two angles and any side (AAS or ASA), or
  • Two sides and a non-included angle (SSA).

OCR note: OCR does not require you to consider the ambiguous case of the sine rule (where two different triangles are possible).

7 The Cosine Rule Higher Tier — All Boards Year 11

The cosine rule also works in any triangle. Use it when you do not have a handy opposite side–angle pair.

Example: Two sides are 7 cm and 9 cm, and the angle between them is 50°. Find the third side.

Here we use: \(a^2 = b^2 + c^2 - 2bc\cos A\) with the 50° angle between the 7 and 9 sides.

Finding a side: \(a^2 = b^2 + c^2 - 2bc \cos A\)

Finding an angle: \(\cos A = \dfrac{b^2 + c^2 - a^2}{2bc}\)

Use the cosine rule when you know:

  • Two sides and the included angle (SAS), or
  • All three sides (SSS).

Important: The cosine rule is not given on your formula sheet — you must memorise it. The sine rule and area formula are provided in the exam.

8 Area of a Triangle Using Sine Higher Tier — All Boards Year 11

If you know two sides and the angle between them, you can find the area without the height.

Example: Triangle with sides 7 cm and 9 cm and included angle 50°.
Area = ½ × 7 × 9 × sin 50° = ½ × 63 × 0.766 = 24.1 cm².

\(\text{Area} = \frac{1}{2}ab \sin C\)

This formula works because \(b \sin C\) is the perpendicular height of the triangle when side \(a\) is the base.

This formula is provided on your exam formula sheet.

9 Trigonometric Graphs Higher Tier — All Boards Year 11

You need to recognise the shapes of sin, cos, and tan from 0° to 360°.

Quick picture in your head: \(\sin 90° = 1\) (top of the wave), \(\cos 0° = 1\) (starts high), \(\tan 45° = 1\) (steep middle).

  • y = sin x: A wave starting at (0,0), rising to a maximum of 1 at 90°, returning to 0 at 180°, reaching −1 at 270°, back to 0 at 360°. The graph repeats every 360°.
  • y = cos x: A wave starting at (0,1), dropping to 0 at 90°, reaching −1 at 180°, returning to 0 at 270°, back to 1 at 360°.
  • y = tan x: A repeating curve with vertical asymptotes at 90°, 270°, etc. It passes through (0,0), (180°,0), (360°,0) and repeats every 180°.

Exam tip: You may be asked to sketch these graphs or to solve equations like \(\sin x = 0.5\) by reading from the graph. Remember there are usually two solutions per cycle.

10 Bearings & Trigonometry All Exam Boards Year 10

A bearing is a direction measured clockwise from North. We always write three figures: 045°, not 45°.

Think of a ship or plane on a map: North is “up”, and you turn clockwise to the bearing.

Bearing questions often mean drawing a diagram with North lines, then using angle facts and trigonometry (or the cosine rule) to find distances.

Typical exam question: "A ship sails 12 km on a bearing of 070°, then 8 km on a bearing of 150°. Find its distance from its starting point."

For these problems, draw the two legs, add North lines, and use angle facts to find the angle between the two paths. Then apply the cosine rule.

11 Angles of Elevation & Depression All Exam Boards Year 10

Angle of elevation: you look up from the horizontal — like spotting a bird in the sky.

Angle of depression: you look down from the horizontal — like standing on a cliff looking at a boat.

12 3D Trigonometry Higher Tier — All Boards Year 11

3D questions look scary, but the trick is to find a right-angled triangle hidden inside the shape.

  1. Spot the right-angled triangle you need (draw it flat on the page if it helps).
  2. Find missing lengths first using Pythagoras or trig on other triangles in the shape.
  3. Then use trig or Pythagoras on your target triangle.

Example: To find the angle between a diagonal of a cuboid and its base, identify the right-angled triangle formed by the diagonal, its projection on the base, and the vertical edge.

These questions look harder than they are. The trick is to always work in 2D triangles drawn from the 3D figure — never try to apply trig directly to the 3D shape.

13 Problem‑Solving Strategy All Exam Boards Year 11

When you get a trig question in an exam, follow this checklist:

  1. Draw a diagram if one isn't provided. Label all known sides and angles.
  2. Decide: right-angled or not? If yes, use Pythagoras or SOH CAH TOA. If no, use sine rule, cosine rule, or area formula.
  3. For non-right-angled triangles:
    • Sine rule: when you have a "pair" (a side and its opposite angle).
    • Cosine rule: when you have SAS or SSS.
    • Area formula: when you need area and have two sides and the included angle.
  4. Check your answer is sensible. The longest side should be opposite the largest angle. Angles in a triangle must sum to 180°.
  5. Watch units: make sure all lengths are in the same unit before calculating.
