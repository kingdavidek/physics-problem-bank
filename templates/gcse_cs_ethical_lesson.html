1 Environmental Impacts Year 10 AQAOCREdexcel
1.1 Energy use and carbon footprint Year 10 AQAOCREdexcel

Digital devices and data centres use electricity 24/7 for computing and cooling. Carbon impact depends on how power is generated (coal vs renewables).

Key points: streaming video, AI training, cloud services and Bitcoin-style mining consume large energy; always-on home devices add up.

Worked example: A school moves files to the cloud. Benefit: fewer local servers. Risk: unknown total energy if the provider runs huge global data centres.

Making phones, laptops and servers needs raw materials (metals, rare earth elements), water and factory energy. E-waste is discarded equipment; toxic substances can leak if dumped irresponsibly.

Worked example: Replacing a whole laptop because one component failed creates more e-waste than repair — Edexcel highlights replacement cycle and disposal.

1.3 Planned obsolescence and recycling Year 10–11 AQAOCREdexcel

Planned obsolescence — products designed to fail or feel outdated quickly (non-replaceable batteries, no updates). Drives more manufacturing and waste.

Mitigation: repair cafés, longer device life, WEEE recycling schemes, buying refurbished kit, reducing unnecessary upgrades.

Worked example: A phone stops receiving security updates after three years. Users buy new phones → more mining and e-waste, even though the screen still works.

2 Legal — Data Protection Year 10–11 AQAOCREdexcel
2.1 Personal data, UK GDPR and DPA 2018 Year 10 AQAOCREdexcel

Personal data identifies a living person (name, email, photo, IP address, school ID). UK GDPR and the Data Protection Act 2018 control how organisations collect, use, store and share it.

Key ideas: lawful basis for processing, purpose limitation, data minimisation (only collect what you need), accuracy, security, storage limitation.

Worked example: A homework app stores pupil names and grades. The school is a data controller; the app company may be a processor — both must keep data secure and lawful.

2.2 Consent, privacy rights and the ICO Year 11 AQAOCREdexcel

Consent must be informed and freely given where required — pre-ticked boxes are often invalid. Individuals have rights including access to their data, correction, and in some cases erasure.

The ICO (Information Commissioner's Office) regulates data protection in the UK and can fine organisations for serious breaches.

Worked example: A newsletter asks “uncheck to refuse marketing” — weak consent. Better: clear opt-in with plain language.

2.3 Data breaches and school responsibilities Year 11 AQAOCREdexcel

A personal data breach is loss, theft or unauthorised access to personal data. Schools must train staff, limit access, encrypt sensitive data where appropriate, and report serious breaches to the ICO within 72 hours when required.

Worked example: A teacher emails a spreadsheet of pupil home addresses to the wrong parent → breach. Steps: contain leak, assess harm, notify ICO if high risk, inform families, review procedures.

3 Legal — Misuse & Intellectual Property Year 10–11 AQAOCREdexcel
3.1 Computer Misuse Act 1990 Year 10 AQAOCR

Makes unauthorised access to computer material illegal, including:

  • Accessing files or accounts without permission
  • Access with intent to commit further crimes
  • Unauthorised modification — viruses, ransomware, deleting files

AQA links hacking (unauthorised access) to this area. Ethical hacking / penetration testing is legal only with written permission.

Worked example: A pupil uses a guessed admin password to change grades → Computer Misuse Act offence, not “just a prank”.

3.2 Copyright, Designs and Patents Act 1988 Year 10 AQAOCREdexcel

Protects original literary, artistic, musical and software works. Copying, sharing or adapting without permission or a licence can infringe copyright.

Allowed in school: use licences (school agreement), Creative Commons, royalty-free assets, or your own work. Always credit sources where required.

Worked example: Downloading a paid game from a torrent site and redistributing it → copyright infringement plus possible CMA offence.

5 Ethical Issues Year 10–11 AQAOCREdexcel
5.1 Privacy: citizens vs government Year 11 AQAOCR
Balanced debate (AQA): Citizens value privacy and may oppose mass surveillance. Governments argue they need data access to prevent terrorism and serious crime. Exams reward both sides.

Worked example: Police request backdoor access to all messaging apps. For: investigate criminals. Against: weakens security for everyone; criminals may use other tools.

5.2 Surveillance, cookies and targeted ads Year 10–11 AQAOCREdexcel

CCTV, tracking cookies, location data and social media profiling raise privacy questions even when legal.

  • Who collects data and for what purpose?
  • Is there informed consent?
  • Could data be misused (leaks, profiling, discrimination)?

Worked example: A shop uses facial recognition for “VIP service” without clear signs — ethical concerns about consent and bias; possible legal issues under data protection.

5.3 AI, automation and algorithmic bias Year 11 AQAEdexcelOCR

Artificial intelligence and automation can improve efficiency but raise issues of accountability, safety, algorithmic bias and legal liability (Edexcel).

Algorithmic bias — unfair outcomes because training data or rules favour one group (e.g. hiring AI trained on past biased decisions).

Worked example: An exam marking AI scores rural schools lower because training data was mostly city schools — biased and unethical; may also be unlawful discrimination.

6 Cultural & Social Impacts Year 10 AQAOCR
6.1 Digital divide and accessibility Year 10 AQAOCR

The digital divide is unequal access to devices, broadband, skills and support. Causes include poverty, rural locations, age, disability and language barriers.

Accessibility: screen readers, subtitles, adjustable text — good design helps everyone; ignoring accessibility excludes disabled users (ethical and sometimes legal).

Worked example: During remote learning, pupils without laptops fall behind — cultural/social inequality widened by technology gaps.

6.2 Online behaviour and cultural change Year 10–11 AQAOCR

Technology changes how people communicate: social media, echo chambers, anonymity, cyberbullying, trolling, misinformation.

Positive: global collaboration, education resources, support communities.

Negative: addiction, mental health pressure, spread of false news, harassment.

Worked example: A viral false rumour damages a pupil's reputation — cultural harm; schools respond with digital citizenship education and reporting tools.

7 Exam Scenarios (AQA-style contexts) Year 11 AQA
7.1 Mobile technology and wireless networking Year 11 AQAOCR

Smartphones enable constant connectivity but increase distraction, location tracking, and e-waste from short upgrade cycles. Wireless networking (Wi‑Fi, 5G) expands access but raises security risks on public networks — see also the Cyber Security topic.

Worked example (6-mark style): Mobile banking — benefit: convenience; legal: biometric data under GDPR; ethical: elderly users without smartphones excluded; environmental: millions of replaced handsets yearly.

7.2 Cloud storage and hacking Year 11 AQA

Cloud storage — files on provider servers: scalability and backup vs privacy, jurisdiction, provider breaches.

Hacking (AQA: unauthorised access) — illegal under Computer Misuse Act; ethical debates about whistle-blowing still require legal analysis.

Worked example: Celebrity photos leaked from cloud backups — legal breach of CMA and GDPR; ethical harm to victims; reputational damage to provider.
