Energy Changes and Rates of Reaction

MYP Chemistry – Energetics and Kinetics

1. Exothermic & Endothermic Reactions

Chemical reactions involve energy changes. They can be classified as:

  • Exothermic – energy is released to the surroundings (ΔH is negative).
  • Endothermic – energy is absorbed from the surroundings (ΔH is positive).

Examples: combustion (exothermic), thermal decomposition (endothermic), neutralisation (exothermic), photosynthesis (endothermic).

In an exothermic reaction, the products have less energy than the reactants; the excess is released. In an endothermic reaction, the products have more energy than the reactants; the extra must be absorbed.

2. Enthalpy and Energy Level Diagrams

Enthalpy (H) is the total heat content of a system. We measure ΔH (change in enthalpy).

3. Bond Energy Calculations

ΔH can be estimated from bond energies:

ΔH = Σ (bond energies broken) − Σ (bond energies made)

If the energy released forming new bonds is greater than the energy absorbed breaking old bonds, the reaction is exothermic.

Example: H₂ + Cl₂ → 2HCl
Bonds broken: H–H (436) + Cl–Cl (242) = 678 kJ
Bonds made: 2 × H–Cl (2 × 431) = 862 kJ
ΔH = 678 − 862 = −184 kJ (exothermic)

4. Calorimetry

Calorimetry measures heat changes in reactions. The basic equation is:

q = m × c × ΔT

  • q = heat energy (J)
  • m = mass of solution (g)
  • c = specific heat capacity (J g⁻¹ °C⁻¹) – for water c = 4.18
  • ΔT = temperature change (°C)

Example: 50 g of water is heated from 20°C to 30°C. q = 50 × 4.18 × 10 = 2090 J (2.09 kJ).

5. Rates of Reaction

The rate of reaction measures how quickly reactants turn into products. It can be increased by:

  • Increasing temperature – particles move faster, collide more often and with more energy.
  • Increasing concentration (or pressure for gases) – more particles per unit volume, more frequent collisions.
  • Increasing surface area – more exposed solid reactant for collisions.
  • Using a catalyst – provides an alternative pathway with lower activation energy.

Rate is often measured by recording the volume of gas produced, change in mass, or colour change over time.

6. Activation Energy & Catalysts

Activation energy (Ea) is the minimum energy needed for particles to react when they collide.

A catalyst speeds up a reaction without being used up. It lowers the activation energy by providing an alternative reaction pathway.

Catalysts do **not** change ΔH or the energy of reactants/products – they only lower the energy barrier.

Enzymes are biological catalysts; metals like platinum and nickel are common industrial catalysts.
