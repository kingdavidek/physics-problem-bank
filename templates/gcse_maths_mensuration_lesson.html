Mensuration

Mensuration means measuring shapes — how long around the edge, how much flat space inside, and how much room a 3D object takes up. We will work with 2D areas, 3D volumes, and shapes made from more than one piece.

1 Perimeter and Area of 2D Shapes All Exam Boards Year 8

Example: A rectangle is 6 cm by 4 cm. Area = 6 × 4 = 24 cm². Perimeter = 6 + 4 + 6 + 4 = 20 cm.

The perimeter is the distance all the way around the edge. The area is how much flat space is inside.

Shape Area Perimeter
Rectangle\(lw\)\(2(l+w)\)
Triangle\(\tfrac{1}{2}bh\)sum of sides
Parallelogram\(bh\) (perpendicular)sum of sides
Trapezium\(\tfrac{1}{2}(a+b)h\)sum of sides
Circle\(\pi r^2\)\(\pi d = 2\pi r\)

Compound shapes: split the shape into rectangles, triangles, or circles, then add the areas. Or subtract a hole. Use the perpendicular height for triangles and trapeziums — not the sloping side unless the question says so.

Units: 1 m² = 10 000 cm². To change cm² to m², divide by 10 000.

2 Circles, Arcs, and Sectors All Exam Boards Year 10

Example: A sector has radius 10 cm and angle 90° at the centre. The full circle circumference is \(2\pi \times 10\). A quarter of that is the arc: \(\frac{90}{360} \times 2\pi \times 10 = \mathbf{5\pi}\) cm.

A sector is a “pizza slice” — two radii and a curved arc. \(\theta\) is the angle at the centre in degrees.

\(\text{Arc length} = \dfrac{\theta}{360} \times 2\pi r\)    \(\text{Sector area} = \dfrac{\theta}{360} \times \pi r^2\)

Going all the way around the sector (perimeter) = arc length + two radii: arc + \(2r\).

Segment: the bit between a chord and an arc. Area = sector area − triangle area, with triangle area = \(\tfrac{1}{2}r^2\sin\theta\).

In exams: Sometimes you must leave π in the answer — only use 3.14 if the question tells you to.

3 Volume of 3D Solids All Exam Boards Year 10

Example: A cylinder has radius 3 cm and height 5 cm. Volume = \(\pi \times 3^2 \times 5 = \mathbf{45\pi}\) cm³ (about 141 cm³ if you use π ≈ 3.14).

Volume is the space inside a 3D shape. For a prism, multiply the area of the end face by how long it is.

Solid Volume formula Key variables
Cuboid\(lwh\)length, width, height
Prism\(A \times l\)cross-section area × length
Cylinder\(\pi r^2 h\)radius, height
Cone\(\tfrac{1}{3}\pi r^2 h\)base radius, perpendicular height
Pyramid\(\tfrac{1}{3} \times \text{base area} \times h\)base area, perpendicular height
Sphere\(\tfrac{4}{3}\pi r^3\)radius
Hemisphere\(\tfrac{2}{3}\pi r^3\)radius (half of sphere)
Frustum\(\tfrac{\pi h}{3}(R^2 + Rr + r^2)\)large radius R, small radius r, height h

Remember: A cone or pyramid is exactly one-third of a cylinder or prism with the same base and perpendicular height — that is why we multiply by \(\tfrac{1}{3}\).

Slant height: If you need the sloping edge \(l\), use Pythagoras: \(l = \sqrt{r^2 + h^2}\) (radius and perpendicular height).

Quick Check

A cuboid is 4 cm × 3 cm × 2 cm. What is its volume?

4 Surface Area of 3D Solids All Exam Boards Year 10

Example: A closed cylinder has \(r = 2\) cm and \(h = 10\) cm. Curved part = \(2\pi rh = 40\pi\). Two circles = \(2\pi r^2 = 8\pi\). Total surface area = \(48\pi\) cm².

Surface area is the total area you would cover if you peeled the shape flat (its net).

Solid Surface area formula Notes
Cuboid\(2(lw + lh + wh)\)3 pairs of identical rectangles
Cylinder (closed)\(2\pi r(r+h)\)curved = 2πrh; two circles = 2πr²
Cone (closed)\(\pi r(r + l)\)l = slant height; curved = πrl
Sphere\(4\pi r^2\)—
Hemisphere (closed)\(3\pi r^2\)curved (2πr²) + flat circle (πr²)
Prism2 × (end area) + (perimeter of end) × ll = length of prism

Slant height: For a cone, find \(l = \sqrt{r^2 + h^2}\) first if you only have \(r\) and perpendicular \(h\).

Open or closed? A tin with no lid is an open cylinder — you do not count the top circle. Read what faces are actually on the outside.

5 Composite Solids Higher Skill Year 11

Example: A silo is a cylinder with a hemisphere on top, both radius 2 m, cylinder height 5 m. Volume = \(\pi r^2 h + \frac{2}{3}\pi r^3 = \pi(4)(5) + \frac{2}{3}\pi(8) = 20\pi + \frac{16}{3}\pi\) m³. Add the two parts — do not double-count the join inside.

A composite solid is two or more shapes stuck together. Volume: add each part. Surface area: only count faces you can touch from outside — not the hidden flat face where they meet.

Hemisphere + cylinder (same radius):

Total volume = \(\pi r^2 h + \dfrac{2}{3}\pi r^3\)

Total surface area = \(2\pi r^2 + 2\pi r h\) (curved hemisphere + curved cylinder; the flat join is inside)

How we tackle these:

  1. Name each shape (cylinder, cone, hemisphere, …).
  2. Work out each volume or face area on its own.
  3. Add volumes. For surface area, leave out any face glued together inside.

A composite solid is made from a cylinder (V = 100 cm³) and a cone (V = 40 cm³) joined at the base. Total volume?

6 Similar Shapes – Length, Area, and Volume Scale Factors Higher Skill Year 10

Example: Two similar cones have lengths in ratio 1 : 3. The smaller has surface area 50 cm². The larger has area \(50 \times 3^2 = \mathbf{450}\) cm² (areas scale with \(k^2\), not \(k\)).

If matching lengths are multiplied by k (the length scale factor), then:

\(\text{Area is multiplied by } k^2 \qquad \text{Volume is multiplied by } k^3\)

Going from big to small? Divide by \(k^2\) or \(k^3\) instead.

In exams: Find \(k\) from a pair of matching lengths first. Then square for area or cube for volume.

Quick Check

Two similar solids have length scale factor 2. The smaller has volume 15 cm³. What is the larger volume?

7 Density, Mass, and Volume All Exam Boards Year 11

Example: A block has density 2.5 g/cm³ and volume 20 cm³. Mass = density × volume = 2.5 × 20 = 50 g.

Density tells you how “heavy” a material is for its size. The link is:

\(\text{Mass} = \text{Density} \times \text{Volume}\)

Use the triangle below: cover the letter you want and read what is left.

You can rearrange to:

  • \(M = D \times V\)
  • \(D = M \div V\)
  • \(V = M \div D\)

In exams: Often you find the volume of a shape first (cylinder, cuboid, …), then multiply by density to get mass.

Quick Check

  • Length × \(k\) · Area × \(k^2\) · Volume × \(k^3\)

  • Prism: area of cross-section × length

  • Cylinder \(2\pi r(r+h)\) · Sphere \(4\pi r^2\)
