Photoelectric Effect and Wave‑Particle Duality

AQA A‑Level Physics – Quantum Phenomena (Section 2.2)

1. The Photoelectric Effect

When light of frequency above a certain value is shone onto a metal surface, electrons are emitted from the surface. These emitted electrons are called photoelectrons.

Why the wave theory failed

Classical wave theory predicted that any frequency of light should eventually cause emission, because the energy absorbed by each electron would accumulate over many wave cycles. Experimentally, however, a threshold frequency exists: below it, no electrons are emitted no matter how intense the light.

The photon model explains this:

  • Light travels as discrete packets (photons), each with energy E = hf.
  • An electron can absorb only one photon at a time.
  • If a single photon has insufficient energy, the electron cannot escape.
  • Increasing intensity means more photons per second, so more electrons are emitted (but only if frequency > threshold).

2. Threshold Frequency & Work Function

The threshold frequency, \(f_0\), is the minimum frequency of light that can cause photoemission for a given metal.

The work function, \(\phi\), is the minimum energy an electron needs to escape from the metal surface. It is related to the threshold frequency by: \[ \phi = hf_0 \]

The potential well model

Electrons in a metal can be thought of as sitting in a potential well — a region of electrostatic attraction. The depth of the well is the work function: the minimum energy needed for an electron to climb out. Electrons at the surface require less energy than those deeper inside, which is why not all emitted electrons have the same kinetic energy.

Key fact: The work function is different for each metal, which is why different metals have different threshold frequencies.

3. Einstein's Photoelectric Equation

Conservation of energy gives the photoelectric equation: \[ hf = \phi + E_{k,\text{max}} \] where:

  • \(hf\) = energy of the incident photon,
  • \(\phi\) = work function (minimum energy to escape),
  • \(E_{k,\text{max}}\) = maximum kinetic energy of the emitted photoelectron.

This equation makes two predictions that match experiment:

  • Below threshold frequency, \(hf < \phi\) → no emission.
  • Above threshold, increasing frequency increases the maximum kinetic energy.

4. Stopping Potential

To measure the maximum kinetic energy, we apply a reverse voltage, called the stopping potential \(V_s\), until the photocurrent drops to zero. At this point, the electrical work done equals the maximum kinetic energy: \[ eV_s = E_{k,\text{max}} \] Combining with Einstein's equation gives a straight‑line relationship: \[ eV_s = hf - \phi \quad\Rightarrow\quad V_s = \frac{h}{e}f - \frac{\phi}{e} \] A graph of \(V_s\) against \(f\) yields a gradient of \(h/e\) and an x‑intercept equal to the threshold frequency.

5. Electron Diffraction & de Broglie Wavelength

If particles have wave‑like properties, they should exhibit diffraction. Louis de Broglie proposed that any moving particle has an associated wavelength given by: \[ \lambda = \frac{h}{p} \qquad (p = \text{momentum}) \] For electrons, this predicts wavelengths similar to X‑rays. In 1927, Davisson and Germer observed electron diffraction from a nickel crystal, confirming the wave nature of matter.

Diffraction is strongest when the wavelength is comparable to the spacing between atoms — exactly what happens in their experiment.

Wave‑particle duality means that light and matter are best described sometimes as waves, sometimes as particles, depending on the situation.

6. Energy Levels and Photon Emission

Electrons in atoms occupy discrete energy levels. They can be excited (move to a higher level) by absorbing exactly the right amount of energy, often from colliding free electrons or photons.

When an excited electron falls back to a lower level, it emits a photon whose energy equals the difference between the two levels: \[ \Delta E = hf \] This produces a line spectrum — only certain wavelengths appear, which act as a fingerprint for the element. For example, fluorescent tubes use mercury vapour; collisions excite mercury atoms, which then emit UV photons, and a phosphor coating converts them to visible light.
