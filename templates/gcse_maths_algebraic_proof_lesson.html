Algebraic Proof

Use algebra to show that a result is always true for every integer, or find a counterexample when it is not.

AQA · Edexcel · OCR Higher Tier
▶ Practice Quiz (10 MCQs)
1 What is algebraic proof? Higher

Checking a few examples on a calculator is not proof. Algebraic proof means:

  • Start with a letter (usually \(n\)) for any integer
  • Manipulate the expression using algebra
  • End with a clear conclusion (e.g. “always even”, “multiple of 3”)

Everyday idea: “All even house numbers on a street add to an even total” — you can explain why with algebra, not by checking every door.

Exam tip: Write conclusion words: “therefore”, “hence”, “so this is always…”. Show every expansion step that earns method marks.
2 Even and odd numbers

Standard forms (any integer \(n\)):

  • Even: \(2n\)   (multiple of 2)
  • Odd: \(2n + 1\)   (one more than an even number)

Prove \(2n+1\) is always odd: it is not divisible by 2 because subtracting 1 gives \(2n\), which is even.

Prove \((2n+1)^2\) is always odd: expand to \(4n^2 + 4n + 1 = 2(2n^2 + 2n) + 1\) — “twice something + 1”.

🧠 Quick Check

Which is always odd for any integer \(n\)?

3 Consecutive integers

Consecutive means “next door” on the number line: \(n\), \(n+1\), \(n+2\), …

Sum of two consecutive: \(n + (n+1) = 2n+1\) → always odd.

Product \(n(n+1)\): always even — one of \(n\) and \(n+1\) must be even.

Sum of three consecutive: \((n-1) + n + (n+1) = 3n\) → always a multiple of 3.

Square gap: \((n+1)^2 - n^2 = n^2 + 2n + 1 - n^2 = 2n+1\) (always odd).

4 Identities and factorising

An identity is true for all values of \(n\) (e.g. expanding both sides shows they match).

Example: Show \(n^2 + n\) is even.

  • Factorise: \(n^2 + n = n(n+1)\)
  • \(n\) and \(n+1\) are consecutive → product is even ✓

Factorising is often the first step in a proof — it reveals structure (consecutive numbers, common factors).

5 Counterexamples

To disprove “always true”, you need one value that fails — a counterexample.

Example: “\(n^2 + 1\) is prime for every positive \(n\)” — false because:

  • \(n = 3\): \(3^2 + 1 = 10 = 2 \times 5\) (not prime)

One counterexample is enough; you do not need to test all integers.

🧠 Quick Check

Which shows “\(n^2\) is always greater than \(n\)” is false?
