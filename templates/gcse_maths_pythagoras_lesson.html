Pythagoras' Theorem

In any right-angled triangle, the square on the hypotenuse equals the sum of the squares on the other two sides. This links algebra, geometry, coordinates, and 3D problems — a core GCSE skill on Foundation and Higher.

1 The Theorem All Exam Boards

Example: A right triangle has shorter sides 3 cm and 4 cm. Then 3² + 4² = 9 + 16 = 25 = 5², so the hypotenuse is 5 cm.

a² + b² = c²   (c is the hypotenuse — longest side, opposite the right angle)

  • a and b are the two shorter sides (legs).
  • c is the hypotenuse — always the longest side.
  • Label the right angle before you start — it tells you which side is c.

2 Finding the Hypotenuse

Example: Shorter sides 6 cm and 8 cm. Find c.

c² = 6² + 8² = 36 + 64 = 100 → c = √100 = 10 cm.

Steps: (1) Square both shorter sides. (2) Add. (3) Square root.

3 Finding a Shorter Side

Example: Hypotenuse 13 cm, one leg 5 cm. Find the other leg.

b² = 13² − 5² = 169 − 25 = 144 → b = 12 cm.

Rearrangement: (shorter side)² = c² − (other shorter side)² — subtract before you square root.

Hypotenuse 10 cm, one leg 6 cm. Other leg?

4 Is the Triangle Right-Angled?

Converse: If a² + b² = c² (with c the longest side), the triangle is right-angled. If not equal, it is not.

Example: Sides 7, 24, 25 → 7² + 24² = 49 + 576 = 625 = 25² ✓ right-angled.

Counter-example: 5, 12, 14 → 5² + 12² = 169 but 14² = 196 ✗ not right-angled.

Common mistake: Checking a + b = c instead of a² + b² = c².

Sides 9 cm, 12 cm, 15 cm — right-angled?

5 2D Applications

Rectangle diagonal: A 9 cm × 12 cm rectangle has diagonal √(9² + 12²) = √225 = 15 cm.

Ladder against a wall: The wall and ground are perpendicular. The ladder is the hypotenuse.

6 3D Pythagoras

For a cuboid with edges l, w, h:

space diagonal² = l² + w² + h²

Example: 3 cm × 4 cm × 5 cm → AG = √(9 + 16 + 25) = √50 ≈ 7.07 cm.

Two-step method: Find a face diagonal first (√(l² + w²)), then use that with h as the legs of a second right triangle.

7 Distance on a Grid

Example: From A(0, 0) to B(6, 8): Δx = 6, Δy = 8 → AB = √(36 + 64) = 10.

AB = √[(x₂ − x₁)² + (y₂ − y₁)²]

Pythagoras — Quick Reference

Core rule

  • \(c^2 = a^2 + b^2\) (\(c\) = hypotenuse)
  • Shorter side: \(a^2 = c^2 - b^2\)
  • Converse: check \(a^2+b^2\) equals longest side squared

Common triples

  • 3-4-5 · 5-12-13 · 8-15-17

Applications

  • Rectangle diagonal: \(\sqrt{w^2+h^2}\)
  • 3D: \(d^2 = l^2 + w^2 + h^2\)
  • Coordinates: \(d = \sqrt{(\Delta x)^2 + (\Delta y)^2}\)

Never add lengths directly — square, add, then square root.
