1 What is a Decimal? All Exam Boards Year 7

A decimal is just a way of writing numbers that are not whole — the part after the decimal point represents a fraction of 1.

Think of money: £3.47 means 3 whole pounds, 4 tens of pence (40p), and 7 pence. That's exactly how decimal place value works.

Look at 3.47:

  • The 3 is in the ones column → worth 3
  • The 4 is in the tenths column → worth \(\frac{4}{10} = 0.4\)
  • The 7 is in the hundredths column → worth \(\frac{7}{100} = 0.07\)

\( 3.47 = 3 + \frac{4}{10} + \frac{7}{100} \)

Moving one place right makes the value 10× smaller. Moving one place left makes it 10× larger.

Key fact: Zeros at the end of a decimal do not change its value. 3.40 = 3.4 = 3.400. But zeros between digits or between the decimal point and a non-zero digit are important: 3.04 ≠ 3.4.

2 Ordering and Comparing Decimals All Exam Boards Year 7

A common mistake is thinking that a number with more digits after the decimal point must be larger. It isn't! The trick is to compare column by column, starting from the left.

Example: Put these in order, smallest to largest: 0.3, 0.29, 0.301, 0.03

Step 1: Add trailing zeros so they all have the same number of decimal places:

0.300   0.290   0.301   0.030

Step 2: Compare the tenths column first. The tenths digits are: 3, 2, 3, 0. The smallest tenths digit is 0, so 0.030 (= 0.03) comes first.

Step 3: Among those with tenths digit 2 or 3: 0.290 has tenths = 2, so it comes next. Then 0.300 and 0.301 both have tenths = 3 — compare hundredths: 0 vs 0. Same! Compare thousandths: 0 vs 1. So 0.300 comes before 0.301.

Correct order: 0.03, 0.29, 0.3, 0.301

Exam tip: If asked to order decimals, always add trailing zeros to make them all the same length first. It makes comparison much easier.

4 Multiplying Decimals All Exam Boards Year 8

The best method for multiplying decimals: ignore the decimal points, multiply as whole numbers, then put the decimal point back.

The rule for placing the decimal point is:

Total decimal places in the answer = sum of decimal places in both numbers you multiplied.

Example: Work out 2.3 × 1.4

  • Ignore decimal points: 23 × 14 = 322
  • Count decimal places: 2.3 has 1 d.p., 1.4 has 1 d.p. → total = 2 d.p.
  • Put point 2 places from the right: 3.22

Example: Work out 0.6 × 0.4

  • Ignore decimal points: 6 × 4 = 24
  • 0.6 has 1 d.p., 0.4 has 1 d.p. → total = 2 d.p.
  • Put point 2 places from the right: 0.24

Example: Work out 3.6 × 0.05

  • 36 × 5 = 180
  • 3.6 has 1 d.p., 0.05 has 2 d.p. → total = 3 d.p.
  • 180 becomes 0.180 = 0.18
⚠️ Sense check: 0.6 × 0.4 should be less than 1 (since both numbers are less than 1), so 0.24 makes sense. If you got 2.4, you placed the decimal point in the wrong spot.

5 Dividing Decimals All Exam Boards Year 8

Dividing by a decimal can look tricky, but there's a simple trick: multiply both numbers by the same power of 10 to turn the divisor into a whole number. The answer doesn't change because you're scaling both sides equally.

Example: Work out 4.8 ÷ 0.6

  • 0.6 has 1 decimal place, so multiply both by 10:
  • 4.8 × 10 = 48,   0.6 × 10 = 6
  • Now do: 48 ÷ 6 = 8

Example: Work out 0.35 ÷ 0.07

  • 0.07 has 2 decimal places, so multiply both by 100:
  • 0.35 × 100 = 35,   0.07 × 100 = 7
  • Now do: 35 ÷ 7 = 5

Example: Work out 7.2 ÷ 0.04

  • Multiply both by 100: 720 ÷ 4 = 180

Dividing a decimal by a whole number is easier — just do standard division and keep the decimal point in the same column.

Example: 6.9 ÷ 3 = 2.3 (same as 69 ÷ 3 = 23, then put the decimal back)

6 Converting Between Fractions and Decimals All Exam Boards Year 8

Fraction → Decimal: A fraction is just a division sum. Divide the top by the bottom.

Example: Convert \(\frac{3}{8}\) to a decimal.

3 ÷ 8 = 0.375

(Work it out: 8 goes into 3.000 → 0.375)

Example: Convert \(\frac{7}{20}\) to a decimal.

\(\frac{7}{20} = \frac{7 \times 5}{20 \times 5} = \frac{35}{100} = \mathbf{0.35}\)

(Tip: if the denominator divides into a power of 10, make an equivalent fraction with 10, 100 or 1000 on the bottom.)

Decimal → Fraction: Write the decimal as a fraction over the appropriate power of 10, then simplify.

Example: Convert 0.35 to a fraction.

\( 0.35 = \frac{35}{100} = \frac{7}{20} \)

Example: Convert 0.125 to a fraction.

\( 0.125 = \frac{125}{1000} = \frac{1}{8} \)

Common ones to memorise:

\(\frac{1}{2} = 0.5\)
\(\frac{1}{4} = 0.25\)
\(\frac{3}{4} = 0.75\)
\(\frac{1}{5} = 0.2\)
\(\frac{1}{8} = 0.125\)
\(\frac{3}{8} = 0.375\)

7 Recurring Decimals All Exam Boards Year 10 Higher

Some fractions produce decimals that go on forever and repeat a pattern. These are called recurring decimals. We show the repeating part with a dot above the first and last digit of the pattern.

For example: \( \frac{1}{3} = 0.\dot{3} = 0.3333\ldots \)   and   \( \frac{1}{7} = 0.\dot{1}4285\dot{7} = 0.142857142857\ldots \)

Converting a recurring decimal to a fraction uses a neat algebra trick. Here's the method step by step:

Example: Write \(0.\dot{6}\) (= 0.6666…) as a fraction.

  • Let \(x = 0.6666\ldots\)
  • Multiply both sides by 10: \(10x = 6.6666\ldots\)
  • Subtract: \(10x - x = 6.6666\ldots - 0.6666\ldots\)
  • \(9x = 6\)
  • \(x = \frac{6}{9} = \mathbf{\frac{2}{3}}\)

Example: Write \(0.\dot{1}\dot{8}\) (= 0.181818…) as a fraction.

  • Let \(x = 0.181818\ldots\)
  • The repeating block "18" has 2 digits, so multiply by 100: \(100x = 18.181818\ldots\)
  • Subtract: \(100x - x = 18\)
  • \(99x = 18\)
  • \(x = \frac{18}{99} = \mathbf{\frac{2}{11}}\)
The pattern: If the repeating block has 1 digit, multiply by 10 and subtract. If it has 2 digits, multiply by 100. If 3 digits, by 1000. Choose your multiplier so the decimal parts line up exactly.

Trickier example: Convert \(0.1\dot{6}\) (= 0.1666…) to a fraction. Here only the 6 repeats — the 1 does not.

  • Let \(x = 0.1666\ldots\)
  • \(10x = 1.666\ldots\) and \(100x = 16.666\ldots\)
  • Subtract: \(100x - 10x = 16.666\ldots - 1.666\ldots\)
  • \(90x = 15\)
  • \(x = \frac{15}{90} = \mathbf{\frac{1}{6}}\)

Practice Strategy Revision

Decimals show up everywhere in GCSE — in money, measurements, probability and standard form. Getting confident here saves you marks across every topic.

Here's a revision checklist. Tick each one off when you're confident:

  • ✅ Place value: Can you state the value of any digit, including thousandths?
  • ✅ Ordering: Do you pad with zeros before comparing?
  • ✅ Adding/subtracting: Do you line up the decimal points every time?
  • ✅ Multiplying: Do you count total decimal places and place the point at the end?
  • ✅ Dividing: Do you multiply both numbers to eliminate the decimal in the divisor?
  • ✅ Fraction ↔ decimal: Can you do 3 ÷ 8 by hand to get 0.375?
  • ✅ Recurring decimals (Higher): Can you use the multiply-and-subtract method?

A great drill: write out the six key fractions (½, ¼, ¾, ⅕, ⅛, ⅜) and their decimal equivalents from memory. You'll be glad you did in the exam.
