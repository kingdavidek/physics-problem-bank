Statistics is all about collecting, showing, and understanding data. Before you draw a chart or find an average, you need to know what type of data you are working with.

1 Types of Data All Exam Boards Year 7

Statistics means working with data — numbers we collect to answer a question.

Example: Shoe sizes in a shop might be 4, 5, 6, 7 only. You cannot have size 4.3. That is discrete data — separate steps, usually whole numbers.

Example: Someone’s height could be 162.4 cm or 175.8 cm — any value in a range. That is continuous data.

Primary data is data you collect yourself (a survey in your class). Secondary data is data someone else already collected (a government website). Secondary data is quick to use, but it might not fit your exact question.

Discrete values are separate “steps”; continuous values can lie anywhere along an interval.

2 Averages: Mean, Median, Mode and Range All Exam Boards Year 8

We use three main averages to summarise data: the mean, median, and mode. The range is not an average — it tells you how spread out the data is.

Example: Test scores: 4, 4, 6, 8, 10 (put in order first).

  • Mean = (4 + 4 + 6 + 8 + 10) ÷ 5 = 6.4
  • Median = middle value = 6
  • Mode = most common = 4
  • Range = highest − lowest = 10 − 4 = 6
MeasureHow to find it
MeanAdd all values and divide by the number of values.
MedianPut values in order and find the middle value.
ModeFind the value that occurs most often.
RangeHighest value minus lowest value.

Common mistake: Always put data in order before you find the median or range.

Here mode (4, most often) and median (6, middle value) differ; the range still spans smallest to largest.

🧠 Quick Check

For the data 4, 4, 6, 8, 10 (in order), what is the median?

3 Frequency Tables All Exam Boards Year 9

A frequency table shows each score (called x) and how many times it happened (frequency f).

Example: 30 students sit a vocab test marked 1–5. Seven students score 2, eight score 3, and so on. Instead of writing all 30 scores out, we count them in a table.

The fx column means “frequency × score” on each row. If 7 students scored 2, that row adds 7 × 2 = 14 to the total of all marks.

Add every fx value: 99. Add every frequency: 30 students. Mean mark = 99 ÷ 30 = 3.3.

\(\displaystyle\text{Mean}=\frac{\sum fx}{\sum f}\)

Here \(\sum f\) is the total number of students (add the frequencies). \(\sum fx\) is the sum of the \(fx\) column. Then divide: \(\text{mean}=99\div30=3.3\) marks (one decimal place is enough for this context).

Exam routine: copy the table carefully, check \(\sum f\) matches the story (here, 30 students), add \(fx\) twice if needed, then \(\sum fx\div\sum f\). If \(x\) is “number of something,” the mean has the same kind of unit (here, “marks”).

🧠 Quick Check

From the table, \(\sum fx = 99\) and \(\sum f = 30\). What is the mean mark?

4 Grouped Frequency Tables All Exam Boards Year 9

Sometimes data is grouped into intervals (e.g. 10–20 cm) because there are too many different values to list one by one.

Example: For the class 10 < x ≤ 20, the numbers go from just above 10 up to 20. The middle is (10 + 20) ÷ 2 = 15. We call 15 the midpoint.

To estimate the mean: multiply each midpoint by its frequency, add them up, then divide by total frequency — same idea as \(\sum fx \div \sum f\), but using midpoints instead of exact scores.

Exam tip: This mean is only an estimate — we do not know the exact values inside each group.

The midpoint sits in the centre of each class; we pretend every value in that class equals the midpoint when estimating the mean.

🧠 Quick Check

What is the midpoint of the class 10 < x ≤ 20?

5 Charts and Diagrams All Exam Boards Year 9

Different charts answer different questions. Pick the one that fits your data.

  • Bar chart — compare separate categories (favourite subjects, colours)
  • Pie chart — show parts of a whole (what fraction chose each option)
  • Line graph — show how something changes over time (temperature each hour)

Common mistake: Bar charts should have gaps between bars because the categories are separate. Histograms use joined bars because they represent continuous grouped data.

🧠 Quick Check

Which chart is best for comparing separate categories like favourite sports?

6 Scatter Graphs and Correlation All Exam Boards Year 10

A scatter graph plots two measurements for each person or object — for example, hours of revision and test score.

What to look for: If points slope upwards as you go right, we say there is positive correlation (more of one tends to go with more of the other). If they slope downwards, it is negative correlation.

You can draw a line of best fit through the middle of the points to make estimates.

  • Interpolation — estimating inside your data range (usually OK)
  • Extrapolation — estimating outside your data range (less reliable)

Exam tip: Correlation does not always mean one thing causes the other. Ice cream sales and sunburn might both rise in summer — sunshine affects both.

Points sloping upward → positive correlation; a line of best fit summarises the trend for estimates.

🧠 Quick Check

On a scatter graph, points rise from left to right. What type of correlation is this?

7 Cumulative Frequency All Exam Boards Year 10 Higher Skill

Cumulative frequency is a running total. Each row adds that group’s frequency to everything above it.

Example: In the table below, 4 students scored 10–20, then 9 more scored 20–30, so after those two groups the cumulative frequency is 4 + 9 = 13.

Plot points at the upper class boundary (top end of each interval) and join them with a smooth S-shaped curve. The curve starts at 0 and ends at the total number of people (n = 40 here).

From the curve you can estimate:

  • Median — read across from \(\frac{n}{2}\) on the cumulative frequency axis.
  • Lower quartile (Q1) — read across from \(\frac{n}{4}\).
  • Upper quartile (Q3) — read across from \(\frac{3n}{4}\).
  • Interquartile range — \(IQR = Q_3 - Q_1\), the spread of the middle 50%.

Worked example: 40 students sit a test. Their scores (out of 60) are recorded below.

Score Frequency Cumulative frequency
\(10 < s \leq 20\)44
\(20 < s \leq 30\)913
\(30 < s \leq 40\)1528
\(40 < s \leq 50\)1038
\(50 < s \leq 60\)240

With \(n = 40\): read at 20 for the median, at 10 for Q1, and at 30 for Q3. The dashed lines on the diagram show exactly how to do this.

Exam routine: draw a horizontal dashed line across from the target cumulative frequency, then drop a vertical line down to the score axis and read off the value. Always show these construction lines in your working.

Common mistake: Plot points at the upper class boundary — not the midpoint. The curve should always start at zero.

8 Box Plots All Exam Boards Year 11 Higher Skill

A box plot (box-and-whisker diagram) shows five key values at a glance — the five-number summary:

  • Minimum — the smallest value (or lower end of the data).
  • Lower quartile Q1 — 25% of data falls below this.
  • Median Q2 — the middle value; 50% below, 50% above.
  • Upper quartile Q3 — 75% of data falls below this.
  • Maximum — the largest value.

The interquartile range \(IQR = Q_3 - Q_1\) measures the spread of the middle 50% of the data. A larger IQR means the data is more spread out in the middle.

Continuing the example from section 7 (40 students' test scores, reading from the CF curve):

Value How found Score
MinimumLower boundary of first class10
Q1Read at \(n/4 = 10\)≈ 27
Median Q2Read at \(n/2 = 20\)≈ 35
Q3Read at \(3n/4 = 30\)= 42
MaximumUpper boundary of last class60
IQR\(Q_3 - Q_1 = 42 - 27\)= 15

The box covers the middle 50% of the data (Q1 to Q3); the whiskers extend to the minimum and maximum; the red line is the median. A box sitting closer to one end shows skewness.

Comparing two box plots: compare the medians first (which group scored higher on average?), then compare the IQRs or overall ranges (which group was more consistent?).

Common mistake: The whiskers end at the minimum and maximum of the data — not at Q1 and Q3. Make sure your box plot matches the scale of the axis underneath.

🧠 Quick Check

From section 7, Q1 ≈ 27 and Q3 = 42. What is the IQR?

9 Histograms All Exam Boards Year 11 Higher Skill

A histogram is for continuous grouped data, especially when class widths are not all the same. The area of each bar shows the frequency — not just the height.

Example: A class has frequency 24 and class width 6.

  • Frequency density = 24 ÷ 6 = 4
  • Draw the bar with height 4 (on a frequency-density axis)

\(\text{Frequency density}=\frac{\text{frequency}}{\text{class width}}\)

If a class has frequency 24 and class width 6, the frequency density is \(24\div6=4\). The bar height should be 4.

Wider classes need a shorter bar for the same frequency; frequency ÷ class width keeps the bar area honest.

Common mistake: In a histogram, the y-axis is frequency density, not frequency, unless all class widths are equal and the question explicitly allows frequency.

🧠 Quick Check

Frequency = 24, class width = 6. What is the frequency density?
