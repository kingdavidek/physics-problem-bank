1 What is a Vector? All Exam Boards Year 10

Example: A car travels at 30 mph north. That is a vector — you know the speed and the direction. A bag weighing 1.5 kg is not a vector — weight alone has no direction.

We use two types of quantity in maths:

  • Scalar: size only (speed without direction, mass, temperature).
  • Vector: size and direction (displacement, velocity, force).

At GCSE you will see vectors in three ways:

  • As a diagram: an arrow — length shows size, arrowhead shows direction.
  • As a column vector: \(\begin{pmatrix} x \\ y \end{pmatrix}\) — top number is horizontal move, bottom is vertical move.
  • As a letter: a bold letter or underlined letter, e.g. a or \(\underline{a}\).

An arrow represents a vector — its length is the magnitude, the arrowhead shows direction.

Same vector, different place: If you slide an arrow without turning or stretching it, it is still the same vector. Two vectors are equal when they have the same length and the same direction.

Which of these is a vector?

2 Column Vectors All Exam Boards Year 10

Example: \(\begin{pmatrix} 3 \\ 2 \end{pmatrix}\) means move 3 right and move 2 up.

A column vector looks like this:

\(\begin{pmatrix} 3 \\ 2 \end{pmatrix}\)

  • Top number (x): horizontal move — positive = right, negative = left.
  • Bottom number (y): vertical move — positive = up, negative = down.

The vector starts at the dot, moves 3 right and 2 up, and the arrow shows the direction.

Do not mix up vectors and coordinates. Coordinates tell you where a point is. Column vectors tell you how to move. They look similar, but they mean different things.

Quick Check

What does \(\begin{pmatrix} -2 \\ 4 \end{pmatrix}\) mean?

2a. Position Vectors All Exam Boards Year 11

Example: Point A is at (3, 4). From the origin O to A, we write \(\overrightarrow{OA} = \begin{pmatrix} 3 \\ 4 \end{pmatrix}\). The arrow means “from O to A”.

Every point on a grid has a position vector. It is the vector from the origin O to that point.

We use position vectors so we can describe points with vector algebra. That helps in harder geometry questions.

Midpoint rule (with numbers): If A has position vector \(\begin{pmatrix} 2 \\ 6 \end{pmatrix}\) and B has \(\begin{pmatrix} 8 \\ 10 \end{pmatrix}\), the midpoint M has position vector \(\dfrac{1}{2}\left(\begin{pmatrix} 2 \\ 6 \end{pmatrix} + \begin{pmatrix} 8 \\ 10 \end{pmatrix}\right) = \begin{pmatrix} 5 \\ 8 \end{pmatrix}\).

\(\overrightarrow{OM} = \dfrac{\mathbf{a} + \mathbf{b}}{2}\)

Quick Check

Point P is at (5, 1). What is \(\overrightarrow{OP}\)?

2b. Displacement Vectors All Exam Boards Year 11

Example: A is at (1, 2) and B is at (5, 7). To go from A to B:

\(\overrightarrow{AB} = \begin{pmatrix} 5-1 \\ 7-2 \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} 4 \\ 5 \end{pmatrix}\)

That means move 4 right and 5 up.

A displacement vector is the move from one point to another — not where the point sits on the grid.

Remember: end minus start.

\(\overrightarrow{AB} = \overrightarrow{OB} - \overrightarrow{OA}\)

Almost every vector geometry question uses this rule at least once. Write it on your formula sheet if it helps.

Quick Check

A is (2, 3), B is (6, 8). Find \(\overrightarrow{AB}\).

3 Adding & Subtracting Vectors All Exam Boards Year 10

Example (add):

\(\begin{pmatrix} 2 \\ 3 \end{pmatrix} + \begin{pmatrix} 4 \\ -1 \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} 2+4 \\ 3+(-1) \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} 6 \\ 2 \end{pmatrix}\)

Example (subtract):

\(\begin{pmatrix} 5 \\ 4 \end{pmatrix} - \begin{pmatrix} 1 \\ 3 \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} 4 \\ 1 \end{pmatrix}\)

With column vectors, add or subtract the top numbers together and the bottom numbers together.

What does adding mean on a diagram? Put the tail of the second vector at the head of the first. The sum is the arrow from start to finish. This is the triangle law of addition. Order does not matter: \(\mathbf{a} + \mathbf{b} = \mathbf{b} + \mathbf{a}\).

4 Multiplying a Vector by a Scalar All Exam Boards Year 10

Example: If \(\mathbf{a} = \begin{pmatrix} 3 \\ -2 \end{pmatrix}\), then:

  • \(2\mathbf{a} = \begin{pmatrix} 6 \\ -4 \end{pmatrix}\) — same direction, twice as long
  • \(-\mathbf{a} = \begin{pmatrix} -3 \\ 2 \end{pmatrix}\) — same length, opposite direction
  • \(\frac{1}{2}\mathbf{a} = \begin{pmatrix} 1.5 \\ -1 \end{pmatrix}\) — same direction, half as long

A scalar is an ordinary number. Multiply every part of the column vector by that number.

If \(\mathbf{b} = k\mathbf{a}\) for some number \(k\), then \(\mathbf{b}\) is parallel to \(\mathbf{a}\). This idea comes up a lot in exam proofs.

Quick Check

If \(\mathbf{v} = \begin{pmatrix} 4 \\ -6 \end{pmatrix}\), what is \(-\frac{1}{2}\mathbf{v}\)?

5 Magnitude of a Vector All Exam Boards Year 10

Example: Find \(|\begin{pmatrix} 3 \\ 4 \end{pmatrix}|\).

Think of a right-angled triangle: horizontal 3, vertical 4, vector is the hypotenuse.

\(|\mathbf{v}| = \sqrt{3^2 + 4^2} = \sqrt{9 + 16} = \sqrt{25} = 5\)

The magnitude is the length of the vector. For \(\begin{pmatrix} x \\ y \end{pmatrix}\):

\(|\mathbf{v}| = \sqrt{x^2 + y^2}\)

We write \(|\mathbf{a}|\) as “the magnitude of \(\mathbf{a}\)”. The answer is always zero or positive — length cannot be negative.

Quick Check

What is \(|\begin{pmatrix} 5 \\ 12 \end{pmatrix}|\)?

6 Parallel Vectors & Collinear Points All Exam Boards Year 11

Parallel vectors

Example: \(\begin{pmatrix} 2 \\ 4 \end{pmatrix} = 2 \times \begin{pmatrix} 1 \\ 2 \end{pmatrix}\), so the two vectors are parallel (same direction, different length).

Two vectors are parallel when one is a scalar multiple of the other: \(\mathbf{b} = k\mathbf{a}\).

If \(k\) is negative, they are parallel but point opposite ways. For example, \(\begin{pmatrix} 2 \\ 4 \end{pmatrix}\) and \(\begin{pmatrix} -1 \\ -2 \end{pmatrix}\).

Collinear points

Points A, B, and C are collinear when they lie on one straight line. To prove it, show:

  1. \(\overrightarrow{AB}\) is parallel to \(\overrightarrow{BC}\) (one is a multiple of the other), and
  2. They share point B (so the lines are the same line, not just parallel).

This type of question appears often on Higher papers.

Quick Check

Which pair of vectors is parallel?

7 Vector Geometry & Finding Paths All Exam Boards Year 11

Exam questions often give you a diagram with labelled vectors. You need to write one vector in terms of others.

Example: Let \(\mathbf{a} = \overrightarrow{OA}\) and \(\mathbf{b} = \overrightarrow{OB}\). Find \(\overrightarrow{OM}\) where M is the midpoint of AB.

  • O to A: \(\mathbf{a}\)
  • A to B: \(\mathbf{b} - \mathbf{a}\)
  • A to midpoint M: \(\frac{1}{2}(\mathbf{b} - \mathbf{a})\)
  • So \(\overrightarrow{OM} = \mathbf{a} + \frac{1}{2}(\mathbf{b} - \mathbf{a}) = \frac{\mathbf{a} + \mathbf{b}}{2}\)
Golden rule: Build a route from where you start to where you want to end, using vectors you already know.

Break the journey into small steps. Add those steps together. This method works for most vector geometry questions.

Quick Check

\(\mathbf{a} = \overrightarrow{OA}\), \(\mathbf{b} = \overrightarrow{OB}\). What is \(\overrightarrow{AB}\)?

8 Using Ratios Along a Line Higher Tier — All Boards Year 11

Example: P divides AB in the ratio AP : PB = 2 : 3. So P is \(\frac{2}{5}\) of the way from A to B (not halfway — closer to A).

If \(\mathbf{a} = \overrightarrow{OA}\) and \(\mathbf{b} = \overrightarrow{OB}\):

\(\overrightarrow{OP} = \frac{3\mathbf{a} + 2\mathbf{b}}{5}\)

When P divides AB in the ratio \(m:n\) (AP : PB), the section formula is:

\(\overrightarrow{OP} = \frac{n\mathbf{a} + m\mathbf{b}}{m + n}\)

Memory tip: The number in front of \(\mathbf{a}\) is the ratio on the other side of P (the PB part, which is 3 when AP:PB = 2:3).

Quick Check

P divides AB with AP : PB = 1 : 3. Which is \(\overrightarrow{OP}\)?

9 Vector Proofs Higher Tier — All Boards Year 11

Vector proofs ask you to show a geometry fact using algebra — for example, three points lie on a line, or two lines are parallel.

Facts you should know:

  • Midpoint of AB: \(\frac{\mathbf{a} + \mathbf{b}}{2}\)
  • Diagonals of a parallelogram bisect each other.
  • The line joining the midpoints of two sides of a triangle is parallel to the third side and half its length.
  • A, B, C are collinear if \(\overrightarrow{AB} = k \cdot \overrightarrow{BC}\) for some scalar \(k\).

How we tackle a proof:

  1. Pick two base vectors (often \(\mathbf{a}\) and \(\mathbf{b}\)) from the diagram.
  2. Write every other vector in terms of those two.
  3. Add, subtract, and multiply by scalars to simplify.
  4. If \(\mathbf{p} = k\mathbf{q}\), the vectors are parallel; if they also share a point, the points are collinear.

Keep your working neat. Label each vector you define so the examiner can follow your steps.

Quick Check

You want to prove three points are collinear. What must you show?

10 Translations Using Vectors All Exam Boards Year 9

Example: Translate every point by \(\begin{pmatrix} 3 \\ -2 \end{pmatrix}\). Point (1, 4) moves to (1+3, 4−2) = (4, 2). The shape slides — it does not turn or flip.

A translation moves every point of a shape the same distance in the same direction. We describe it with a column vector.

If the vector is \(\begin{pmatrix} 3 \\ -2 \end{pmatrix}\), then (x, y) → (x + 3, y − 2). At GCSE, translations are the transformation that uses column vectors most directly.

Quick Check

Shape point (2, 5) is translated by \(\begin{pmatrix} -1 \\ 4 \end{pmatrix}\). Where does it go?

11 The Zero Vector & Negative Vectors All Exam Boards Year 10

Example: If \(\mathbf{a} = \begin{pmatrix} 2 \\ 3 \end{pmatrix}\), then \(-\mathbf{a} = \begin{pmatrix} -2 \\ -3 \end{pmatrix}\). Same length, opposite direction.

The zero vector \(\begin{pmatrix} 0 \\ 0 \end{pmatrix}\) has length zero. Adding it changes nothing: \(\mathbf{v} + \mathbf{0} = \mathbf{v}\). Subtracting a vector from itself gives zero: \(\mathbf{a} - \mathbf{a} = \mathbf{0}\).

For displacement: \(\overrightarrow{BA} = -\overrightarrow{AB}\). The trip from B back to A is the reverse of A to B.

Quick Check

If \(\overrightarrow{AB} = \begin{pmatrix} 4 \\ -1 \end{pmatrix}\), what is \(\overrightarrow{BA}\)?
