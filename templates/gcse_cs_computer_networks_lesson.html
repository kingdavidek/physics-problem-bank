1 Types of Networks Year 10 AQAOCREdexcel
1.1 LAN, WAN and PAN Year 10
  • LAN (Local Area Network) — one site, e.g. school, home (high speed, privately owned)
  • WAN (Wide Area Network) — large area, often uses rented lines; the internet is the biggest WAN
  • PAN (Personal Area Network) — devices around one person; AQA expects Bluetooth as the PAN example (headphones, phone to watch)

Why networks? Share files and printers, central backups, communication, access to the internet. Disadvantages: cost, security risks, dependency if the network fails.

Example: PCs in your school computer room = LAN. Your school’s connection to the internet = link from LAN to WAN.

1.2 Client–server and peer-to-peer Year 10 OCREdexcel

Client–server: powerful servers provide services; clients request them. Example: web browser (client) asks a web server for a page.

Peer-to-peer (P2P): devices share roles — no central server required. Example: small office file sharing or some video call setups.

Worked example: School email — client laptops connect to a dedicated mail server (client–server). A LAN party game with no central host can be P2P.

1.3 Wired and wireless Year 10 AQAEdexcel

Wired (Ethernet over copper or fibre) — often faster and more stable; harder to tap casually; cables cost money to install.

Wireless (Wi‑Fi, Bluetooth) — flexible, no cables; can suffer interference, shorter range, shared bandwidth; needs encryption (WPA2/WPA3).

Network speed is measured in bits per second (bps, Mbps, Gbps) — how much data can move in one second.

2 Network Topologies Year 10 AQAOCREdexcel
2.1 Star and bus Year 10 AQAEdexcel

Star — every device connects to a central switch or WAP. If one cable fails, only that device is affected. Most school and home networks are star-shaped logically.

Bus — devices share one backbone cable. Cheap but the backbone is a single point of failure; older Ethernet used this layout.

2.2 Mesh and reliability Year 11 OCREdexcel

Mesh — devices have multiple links to others. If one link breaks, data can use another path. Used where reliability is critical (some backbone links); expensive to wire fully.

Ring — devices in a circle; data passes token-style (less common in new GCSE setups).

Example: Two city offices with two fibre links between them = partial mesh for backup if one cable is cut.

3.2 Switch vs hub Year 11

A hub repeats traffic to every port (collision, wasted bandwidth). A switch learns which MAC address is on which port and sends data only there.

Example: In a classroom of 30 PCs, a switch stops one student’s traffic flooding everyone else’s machines.

4.2 TCP, UDP and layers Year 11 AQAEdexcel

Four-layer TCP/IP model (AQA and Edexcel):

  1. Application — HTTP, HTTPS, SMTP, IMAP, FTP, DNS
  2. Transport — TCP (reliable) or UDP (fast, no guarantee)
  3. Internet — IP addresses and routing
  4. Link — Ethernet, Wi‑Fi (WLAN); NIC and drivers sit here

Example: Loading a secure site: HTTPS → over TCP → over IP → over WiFi/Ethernet.

5 IP Addresses & DNS Year 11 OCREdexcel
5.1 IPv4 and MAC addresses Year 11 OCREdexcel

IPv4 — four numbers 0–255, e.g. 192.168.0.10. Used for routing across networks.

MAC address — 48-bit hardware address on the NIC (e.g. 00:1A:2B:3C:4D:5E). Used on the local link.

Worked example: Your laptop MAC reaches the switch; the router uses IP to send packets to 142.250.x.x (Google).

Private ranges (home LAN): 192.168.x.x, 10.x.x.x — shared one public IP via NAT on the router.

6 Packets, Cloud & Security Year 11 AQAOCREdexcel
6.1 Packet switching Year 11 OCREdexcel

Large files are split into packets with headers (source/destination IP, sequence info). Routers forward packets; they may take different routes and are reassembled at the end.

Example: A video stream is thousands of packets; if one is lost, only that packet is resent (TCP) or skipped (UDP).

6.2 Network security and cloud Year 11 AQAEdexcel

Network security methods work together (defence in depth):

  • Authentication — prove identity before access (password, 2FA)
  • Encryption — HTTPS encrypts web traffic in transit
  • Firewall — filters traffic by rules; blocks unauthorised access
  • MAC address filtering — allow/block devices by hardware address (can be spoofed; not enough alone)

Edexcel also covers penetration testing and ethical hacking to find weaknesses. See the Cyber Security lesson for malware and social engineering in depth.

Cloud services run on remote servers over the internet (storage, email, apps) — OCR links DNS, hosting and web servers to this model.

Worked example: School firewall blocks gaming sites; Wi‑Fi uses WPA3; students submit homework via HTTPS portal.
