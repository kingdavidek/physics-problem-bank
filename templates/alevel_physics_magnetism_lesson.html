Magnetic Fields and Electromagnetism

AQA A-Level Physics — Section 7.5

1. The Motor Effect

A magnetic field is created by moving charges (currents). Field lines run from North to South and show the direction of force on a North-seeking pole. The closer the lines, the stronger the field.

Magnetic Flux Density \(B\): The force per unit current per unit length on a wire at right angles to the field. Measured in Tesla (T), where \(1\,\text{T} = 1\,\text{kg\,s}^{-2}\text{A}^{-1}\).

The Motor Effect: A current-carrying conductor in a magnetic field experiences a force. If the wire is at angle \(\theta\) to the field:

\[ F = BIl\sin\theta \]

The force is maximum when the wire is perpendicular to the field (\(\theta = 90°\)), and zero when parallel (\(\theta = 0°\)).

Fleming's Left-Hand Rule: Point the First finger in the direction of the Field, the seCond finger in the direction of the Current — the thuMb gives the direction of Motion (force).

2. Moving Charges in Magnetic Fields

A single particle of charge \(Q\) moving at velocity \(v\) at angle \(\theta\) to a field \(B\) experiences:

\[ F = BQv\sin\theta \]

Derivation: Current is just moving charge, so \(I = Q/t\). The length of wire can be written as \(l = vt\). Substituting into \(F = BIl\):

\[ F = B \cdot \frac{Q}{t} \cdot vt = BQv \]

The magnetic force is always perpendicular to velocity, so it does no work and the speed remains constant. This force acts as a centripetal force, causing circular motion:

\[ BQv = \frac{mv^2}{r} \implies r = \frac{mv}{BQ} \]

The period of one full orbit is:

\[ T = \frac{2\pi m}{BQ} \]

Notice that \(T\) is independent of speed — this is the principle behind the cyclotron.

3. Cyclotrons

A cyclotron is a particle accelerator made of two D-shaped electrodes called Dees, with a magnetic field perpendicular to their plane and an alternating voltage across the gap between them.

  • Particles spiral outward, gaining energy each time they cross the gap.
  • The magnetic field keeps them in a circular orbit inside each Dee.
  • Because \(T = 2\pi m / BQ\) is independent of speed, the alternating voltage can stay at a fixed frequency (the cyclotron frequency).
  • As speed increases, the radius of the path increases: \(r = mv/BQ\).

Applications include producing radioactive tracers for medical imaging (PET scans) and ion beams for radiotherapy.

4. Magnetic Flux and Flux Linkage

Magnetic Flux \(\Phi\): The total field passing through a surface of area \(A\). Measured in Webers (Wb), where \(1\,\text{Wb} = 1\,\text{T\,m}^2\).

\[ \Phi = BA\cos\theta \]

Here \(\theta\) is the angle between the normal to the surface and the field direction. Flux is maximum when the field is perpendicular to the surface (\(\theta = 0°\)) and zero when parallel (\(\theta = 90°\)).

Flux Linkage \(N\Phi\): For a coil of \(N\) turns, each turn links the same flux:

\[ N\Phi = BAN\cos\theta \]

For a rotating coil with angular speed \(\omega\), the angle changes with time so \(\theta = \omega t\):

\[ N\Phi = BAN\cos(\omega t) \]

5. Electromagnetic Induction

An e.m.f. is induced whenever magnetic flux linkage changes. There are two laws:

  • Faraday's Law: The induced e.m.f. equals the rate of change of flux linkage.
  • Lenz's Law: The induced current opposes the change that caused it (conservation of energy).
\[ \varepsilon = -\frac{\Delta(N\Phi)}{\Delta t} \]

The minus sign encodes Lenz's law — the induced e.m.f. acts to oppose the flux change.

Motional e.m.f. derivation

A conductor of length \(l\) moving at speed \(v\) perpendicular to field \(B\) sweeps out area \(A = lvt\) in time \(t\). The flux change is \(\Delta\Phi = Blvt\), so:

\[ \varepsilon = \frac{\Delta\Phi}{\Delta t} = Blv \]

Rotating coil

For a coil rotating at angular speed \(\omega\), flux linkage varies as \(N\Phi = BAN\cos(\omega t)\). Differentiating with respect to time gives the induced e.m.f.:

\[ \varepsilon = BAN\omega\sin(\omega t) \]

This is sinusoidal — the basis of the alternating current generator.

6. Alternating Current

Alternating current changes direction sinusoidally. Key quantities:

  • Peak voltage \(V_0\): Maximum voltage from equilibrium.
  • Peak-to-peak voltage: Distance from minimum to maximum.
  • RMS voltage \(V_{rms}\): The equivalent DC voltage that delivers the same power.
\[ V_{rms} = \frac{V_0}{\sqrt{2}}, \qquad I_{rms} = \frac{I_0}{\sqrt{2}} \]

UK mains supply is 230 V rms, meaning the peak voltage is \(230\sqrt{2} \approx 325\,\text{V}\).

7. Transformers and Power Transmission

A transformer changes alternating voltage using two coils wound around a laminated soft iron core. The alternating primary current creates a changing flux, inducing an e.m.f. in the secondary coil by Faraday's law.

Transformer equation (derived from equal rates of flux change in each turn):

\[ \frac{V_s}{V_p} = \frac{N_s}{N_p} \]

Ideal transformer (power in = power out):

\[ V_p I_p = V_s I_s \implies \frac{I_s}{I_p} = \frac{N_p}{N_s} \]

Energy losses and how to reduce them

  • Eddy currents — induced in the core, waste energy as heat. Reduced by laminating the core (layers of iron insulated from each other).
  • Coil resistance — heating in the wire. Reduced by using thick copper wire.
  • Magnetisation of core — energy needed to repeatedly magnetise/demagnetise. Reduced by using soft iron (easy to magnetise and demagnetise).

Power transmission

Power lost in cables is \(P = I^2R\). By stepping voltage up (reducing current), losses are dramatically reduced. That is why the National Grid transmits at hundreds of kilovolts.
