Bearings

A bearing tells you which way to go, like on a map or when a pilot gives a heading. You measure the angle clockwise from North. We use bearings in navigation, maps, and exam geometry questions.

1 What Is a Bearing? All Exam Boards Year 9

Example: You stand at point A and face due East. You turned 90° clockwise from North, so the bearing is written 090° (always three digits).

Three rules you must follow:

  1. Measure clockwise from North.
  2. Write three digits (e.g. 008°, 045°, 270°).
  3. Start from the North line at the place you stand — not at the other town.

“Bearing of B from A” means: stand at A, face North, turn clockwise until you point at B. That angle is the bearing.

Direction Bearing Direction Bearing
North 000° South 180°
North-East 045° South-West 225°
East 090° West 270°
South-East 135° North-West 315°

Compass notation (older style): N40°E → 040° · S30°W → 210° · N25°W → 335° · S15°E → 165°

2 Back Bearings (Reverse Bearings) All Exam Boards Year 10

Example: The bearing of B from A is 065°. To travel back from B to A, add 180°: 065° + 180° = 245°.

The back bearing is the bearing for the return trip (opposite direction):

If bearing < 180°:   back bearing = bearing + 180°
If bearing ≥ 180°:   back bearing = bearing − 180°

Same idea: turn around = add or subtract 180°.

More examples:

  • 070° → back bearing 250°
  • 150° → back bearing 330°
  • 200° → back bearing 020°
  • 315° → back bearing 135°

Why it works: North lines at A and B are parallel. Line AB cuts across them like a transversal, so alternate angles match — that is the same as adding 180° to the bearing.

3 Parallel North Lines and Angle Facts All Exam Boards Year 10

Example: Bearing of B from A is 063°. At B, the alternate angle between line BA and the South line is also 63°, so the back bearing is 63° + 180° = 243°.

Every North line points the same way, so North lines at different places are parallel. When path AB crosses them, you can use parallel-line angle facts:

  • Alternate angles (Z-shape) — equal. This links the bearing at A to the angle at B.
  • Co-interior angles (C-shape) — add to 180°.

In exams: You may be told to “use angle facts” instead of just adding 180°. Draw the North lines and mark the Z-shape alternate angles.

4 Trigonometry with Bearings All Exam Boards Year 10

Example: A boat sails 50 km on bearing 090° (due East). East = \(50\sin 90° = 50\) km. North = \(50\cos 90° = 0\) km — it does not move north or south.

If you travel distance \(d\) on bearing \(\theta\), split the journey into East and North parts:

\(\text{East} = d\sin\theta \qquad \text{North} = d\cos\theta\)

If North comes out negative, you went south. If East is negative, you went west. Check the quadrant before you write the final bearing.

To find the bearing when you know East and North:

\(\theta = \arctan\!\left(\dfrac{\text{East}}{\text{North}}\right)\) — then adjust for the correct quadrant

Another example: Bearing 135° for 60 km:

  • East = \(60\sin 135° \approx 42.4\) km
  • North = \(60\cos 135° \approx -42.4\) km (about 42.4 km south)

5 Multi-Leg Journeys All Exam Boards Year 11

Example: Leg 1: 40 km on 050°. Leg 2: 30 km on 140°. Work out East and North for each leg, add them, then use Pythagoras for the straight-line distance from start to finish.

When a journey has several legs, we split each leg into East and North, then combine:

  1. Each leg: East = \(d\sin\theta\), North = \(d\cos\theta\)
  2. Add all East values; add all North values
  3. Resultant distance = \(\sqrt{E_{\text{total}}^2 + N_{\text{total}}^2}\)
  4. Resultant bearing = \(\arctan(E_{\text{total}} / N_{\text{total}})\) — check the quadrant

Return trip: use the opposite East and North (multiply both by −1), then find the bearing.

Quick Check

6 Sine and Cosine Rule with Bearings Higher Tier Year 11

Example: Two boats leave port O. One goes on 030° for 80 km, the other on 090° for 60 km. Angle at O = 090° − 030° = 60°. Then use the cosine rule to find how far apart they are.

When two paths start from the same point on different bearings, they make a triangle. If there is no right angle, use the sine rule or cosine rule.

Cosine rule: \(c^2 = a^2 + b^2 - 2ab\cos C\)    Sine rule: \(\dfrac{a}{\sin A} = \dfrac{b}{\sin B}\)

Angle at the port: subtract the two bearings (e.g. 130° − 040° = 90°). If the gap is over 180°, use \(360°\) minus the difference instead.

Full working: \(AB^2 = 80^2 + 60^2 - 2 \times 80 \times 60 \times \cos 60° = 5200\), so \(AB \approx \mathbf{72.1}\) km.
