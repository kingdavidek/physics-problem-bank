Probability

GCSE Mathematics – Foundation and Higher Tier · All Exam Boards

AQA Edexcel OCR
▶ Practice Quiz (10 MCQs)

Probability tells us how likely something is to happen. In this topic we work with fractions, decimals, and percentages — and we use diagrams when problems get trickier.

1 The Probability Scale All Exam Boards Year 7

Probability is a number that shows how likely an event is. It always sits between 0 and 1.

Think about it: The chance of snow in the Sahara in July is basically 0 (impossible). The chance that a coin lands heads or tails is 1 (certain — one of them must happen).

In the middle, 0.5 means an even chance — like flipping a fair coin and getting heads.

You can write the same probability in different ways: ½ = 0.5 = 50%.

Check your work: If you get a probability below 0 or above 1, go back — something has gone wrong.

🧠 Quick Check

What is the probability of an event that cannot happen?

2 Single-Event Probability All Exam Boards Year 8

When every outcome is equally likely (like a fair die or a fair coin), we count how many outcomes we want and divide by how many outcomes there are in total.

Example: You roll a fair six-sided die. What is the probability of an even number?

  • Even numbers on the die: 2, 4, 6 → 3 favourable outcomes
  • Total outcomes: 1, 2, 3, 4, 5, 6 → 6 outcomes
  • Probability = 3 ÷ 6 = ½

In general, when outcomes are equally likely:

\[ \text{Probability} = \frac{\text{number of favourable outcomes}}{\text{total number of outcomes}} \]

Common mistake: Count how many outcomes there are, not how big the numbers are. Rolling a 6 is not more likely than rolling a 1 on a fair die — each face has the same chance.

🧠 Quick Check

A fair die is rolled. What is P(even number)?

3 The Complement Rule All Exam Boards Year 9

The complement of an event means “it does not happen”. Either the event happens or it does not — so the two probabilities add up to 1.

Example: The probability a bus is late is 0.18. What is the probability it is not late?

  • Not late = everything except late
  • 1 − 0.18 = 0.82

So there is an 82% chance the bus is on time (or early).

\[P(\text{not A}) = 1 - P(\text{A})\]

Exam tip: If “not” is awkward to count directly, use 1 − P(event) — it is often much quicker.

🧠 Quick Check

P(late) = 0.18. What is P(not late)?

4 Expected Frequency All Exam Boards Year 9

Expected frequency tells you roughly how many times you would expect something to happen if you repeated a trial lots of times.

Example: You have a 3/10 chance of winning each time you play a game. You play 200 games. How many wins would you expect?

  • Multiply probability by number of trials: 3/10 × 200 = 60
  • We expect about 60 wins — but in real life you might get a bit more or less.

\[\text{Expected frequency} = \text{probability} \times \text{number of trials}\]

“Expected” does not mean guaranteed. With only a few trials, results can look quite different from the expected value.

🧠 Quick Check

P(win) = 3/10. You play 200 games. How many wins do you expect?

5 Relative Frequency All Exam Boards Year 9

When you actually do an experiment (spin a spinner, flip a coin many times), you can estimate probability from your results. That estimate is called relative frequency.

Example: A spinner lands on blue 46 times in 100 spins. What is the relative frequency of blue?

  • 46 successes out of 100 trials
  • Relative frequency = 46 ÷ 100 = 0.46

\[\text{Relative frequency} = \frac{\text{number of successful outcomes}}{\text{number of trials}}\]

The more trials you do, the closer relative frequency usually gets to the true probability.

🧠 Quick Check

Blue appears 46 times in 100 spins. What is the relative frequency of blue?

6 Mutually Exclusive Events All Exam Boards Year 10

Two events are mutually exclusive if they cannot happen at the same time. On one roll of a die, you cannot roll a 2 and a 5 together.

Example: What is the probability of rolling a 1 or a 6 on a fair die?

  • P(1) = 1/6
  • P(6) = 1/6
  • Add them: 1/6 + 1/6 = 2/6 = 1/3

For mutually exclusive events A and B:

\[P(A \text{ or } B) = P(A) + P(B)\]

Only add probabilities when the events cannot overlap. If they can both happen, you need a different method (tree diagrams or Venn diagrams).

🧠 Quick Check

A fair die is rolled once. What is P(1 or 6)?

7 Tree Diagrams All Exam Boards Year 10 Higher Skill

Tree diagrams help when you have two or more stages — like flipping two coins or picking two cards without replacement.

Example: You flip two fair coins. What is the probability of two heads?

  • First coin: P(H) = 1/2
  • Second coin: P(H) = 1/2
  • Multiply along the branch: 1/2 × 1/2 = 1/4

Another example: Probability of exactly one head (HT or TH):

  • P(HT) = 1/4 and P(TH) = 1/4
  • Add the routes: 1/4 + 1/4 = 1/2

Rules: multiply along a branch; add separate branches that give what you want.

Follow one path from left to right. Multiply the fractions on the branches. The green route is two heads: ½ × ½ = ¼.

Exam tip: Without replacement, the second-stage probabilities often change because there are fewer items left.

🧠 Quick Check

Two fair coins are flipped. What is P(two heads)?

8 Venn Diagrams and Conditional Probability All Exam Boards Year 11 Higher Skill

Venn diagrams sort people or objects into overlapping groups. Each circle is a set. Where the circles overlap, someone belongs to both sets at once.

Example: In a year group of 120 students:

  • 30 study French only
  • 10 study both French and Spanish
  • 15 study Spanish only
  • 65 study neither language

Check: 30 + 10 + 15 + 65 = 120 ✓  |French| = 30 + 10 = 40.  |Spanish| = 10 + 15 = 25.

Each number sits in one region only. The rectangle is the whole year group; the circles are French and Spanish.

How to read it:

  • The overlap (10) is students in both F and S — that is \(F \cap S\).
  • French only (30) is in F but not S.
  • Everyone inside the blue circle (30 + 10) studies French — that is 40 students in total.
  • The 65 outside both circles study neither language.

Conditional probability means “the probability of A given that B has already happened”. The words “given that” tell you to use a smaller group as your starting point — not the whole year group.

Same example, with a condition: You pick a student from those who study French. What is P(they also study Spanish)?

On the Venn diagram below, the green dashed box shows your new sample space: only the French circle (French only + both). Ignore everyone outside that box.

The green box is the only part we use for P(Spanish | French): 40 students in total, 10 of whom also study Spanish.

  • Your denominator is 40 (inside the green box), not 120
  • Of those 40, 10 also study Spanish (the overlap inside the box)
  • So P(Spanish | French) = 10 ÷ 40 = 1/4

Common mistake: When you see “given”, shrink the sample space to the group you are told about — do not use the whole school or year unless the question says so.

🧠 Quick Check

40 students study French; 10 of them also study Spanish. A student is chosen from those who study French. What is P(also studies Spanish)?
