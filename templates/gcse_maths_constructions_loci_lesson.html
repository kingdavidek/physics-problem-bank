Constructions & Loci

Learn how to draw accurate shapes with ruler and compasses, and how to find loci — the paths or regions where points must lie.

1 The Four Basic Loci All Exam Boards Year 10

Example: “All points exactly 5 cm from point P.” You draw a circle with centre P and radius 5 cm. That circle is the locus.

A locus (say “locus”, plural loci) is all the points that fit one rule. GCSE exams use four main types:

① Fixed distance from a point

Locus = circle with centre P and radius r

② Equidistant from two points

Locus = perpendicular bisector of AB

③ Equidistant from two lines

Locus = angle bisector(s) at the intersection

④ Fixed distance from a segment

Locus = stadium: parallel lines joined by semicircles

In exams: Name the locus clearly — e.g. “circle, centre P, radius 5 cm” or “perpendicular bisector of AB”.

2 Constructing the Perpendicular Bisector All Exam Boards Year 10

Example: You need the locus “equidistant from A and B.” We draw the perpendicular bisector of AB — every point on that line is the same distance from A as from B.

Steps

  1. Set compasses to more than half of AB.
  2. From A, draw arcs above and below the line, connecting to points P and Q on either side of AB.
  3. Keep the same radius with your compass; now from B, draw arcs on the other side of the perpendicular bisector (in green) that also connect points P and Q.
  4. Join the two crossing points with a ruler — that line is the perpendicular bisector.
Key property: Every point on the perpendicular bisector is equidistant from A and B.
Use it to find: Circumcentre (intersect perp bisectors of two sides of a triangle)

3 Constructing the Angle Bisector All Exam Boards Year 10

Example: Two paths meet at corner B. The locus “same distance from both paths” is the angle bisector — we construct it with compasses at B.

Steps

  1. Put the compass on B; draw an arc cutting both arms. Call the cuts X and Y.
  2. From X, draw an arc inside the angle.
  3. From Y, with the same radius, draw an arc crossing the first at Z.
  4. Draw the line BZ — that is the angle bisector.
Key property: Every point on the angle bisector is equidistant from both arms of the angle.
Use it to find: Incentre (intersect angle bisectors of two angles of a triangle)

Arcs from X and Y cross at two points — mark Z inside the angle (where the bisector meets the wedge), not the other intersection.

Quick Check

“Equidistant from two lines that meet at B.” What do you construct?

4 Constructing Special Angles (60°, 90°, 30°, 45°) All Exam Boards Year 10

Example: Start with radius 6 cm. Mark A and X on a line with AX = 6 cm. Arcs from A and X with radius 6 cm meet at Y. Triangle AXY is equilateral, so angle at A is 60°.

60° — Equilateral triangle

90° at a point on a line

5 Combined Loci and Regions All Exam Boards Year 11

Example: “Closer to A than B and within 6 cm of B.” Draw the perpendicular bisector of AB (A’s side) and a circle radius 6 cm centre B. Shade where both rules apply.

Exam questions often combine two rules with AND (must satisfy both) or OR (either will do). The method is always the same at first:

  1. Work out each locus on its own.
  2. Draw them on one diagram.
  3. Decide what the question wants: a region to shade, or specific points where the loci meet.

If one rule says “closer to A than B” and another “within 6 cm of B”, the overlap is usually a region (infinitely many points). If both rules are exact distances from A and B — a classic treasure-hunt setup — each rule is a circle, and “both at once” means the circles’ intersection points (0, 1, or 2 locations).

Closer to A AND within 6 cm of B

The shaded lens region is the answer: left half of circle B.

Example: treasure hunt (two exact distances)

A treasure is buried exactly 5 km from post A and exactly 7 km from post B. Each clue is a locus (a circle). The treasure must lie on both circles — so draw them and mark where they cross.

Two circles can meet in 0, 1, or 2 points. Here there are two — you need extra information to pick one. Contrast the left: “closer to A and within 6 cm of B” shades a whole region, not just isolated points.

6 Scale Drawings All Exam Boards Year 9

Example: Scale 1 : 25 000. A road is 4.8 cm on the map. Actual length = \(4.8 \times 25\,000 = 120\,000\) cm = 1.2 km.

Scale formulae
Actual length = Map length × Scale number
Map length = Actual ÷ Scale number
For areas:
Actual area = Map area × Scale²
Bearings on maps: Measure clockwise from North. Write three digits (045°, not 45°).

More examples

Scale 1:25000. Road = 4.8 cm on map. Find actual length.
Actual = 4.8 × 25000 = 120 000 cm
= 1200 m = 1.2 km
Scale 1:50000. Field = 6 cm² on map. Find actual area.
Actual = 6 × 50000² = 6 × 2.5×10⁹ cm²
= 1.5×10¹⁰ cm² = 1.5 km²

7 Circumcircle and Incircle of a Triangle All Exam Boards Year 11

Every triangle has two special circles tied to constructions you already know:

  • Circumcircle — the unique circle that passes through all three vertices (A, B and C). Its centre is the circumcentre O. The triangle sits inside the circle; each vertex touches the circumference.
  • Incircle — the largest circle that fits inside the triangle, touching each side without crossing it. Its centre is the incentre I. The circle sits inside the triangle; each side is a tangent.

Circumcentre ↔ perpendicular bisectors (equidistant from pairs of vertices). Incentre ↔ angle bisectors (equidistant from pairs of sides). O may lie outside an obtuse triangle; I is always inside.

Circumcircle

What it is: Circle through A, B and C — vertices lie on the circumference.
Construction: Perpendicular bisectors of two sides meet at O. Compass radius OA (= OB = OC).
Uses: Finding a point equidistant from all three vertices (e.g. equal-distance mast from three towns).

Incircle

What it is: Circle tangent to all three sides — sits inside the triangle.
Construction: Angle bisectors of two angles meet at I. Compass radius = perpendicular distance from I to any side (inradius r).
Formula: \(r = \dfrac{\text{area}}{\text{semi-perimeter}}\). I is always inside the triangle.

8 Advanced Loci (Higher) All Exam Boards Year 11

Higher-tier questions sometimes use loci that are not straight lines or simple arcs. Three useful types:

  • Sliding ladder — fixed length between wall and floor; the midpoint follows a quarter-circle.
  • Apollonius circle — points where PA : PB = k (a fixed ratio, not equal distance).
  • PA² − PB² = constant — points where the difference of squared distances is fixed; the locus is a straight line perpendicular to AB.
Midpoint of a sliding ladder

A 5 m ladder touches a wall and the floor. The bottom slides along the floor and the top slides down the wall, but the length stays 5 m. Track the midpoint M.

Why a quarter-circle: corner C, ladder length L. Midpoint M satisfies CM = L/2 by Pythagoras on half the ladder. M stays 2.5 m from C → locus is a quarter-circle of radius L/2 in the corner.

Apollonius circle (PA : PB = k)

Find points P where distance to A is half the distance to B (ratio 1 : 2). This is not a perpendicular bisector — the locus is a circle.

Condition: PA/PB = k (k ≠ 1). Set PA² = k²·PB² and expand — the y terms cancel if A and B share a height, giving a circle. A lies on this circle when k < 1; check which arc satisfies the ratio.

PA² − PB² = constant

A different condition: squared distances differ by a fixed amount k. The locus is a straight line (not a circle), perpendicular to AB.

Expand: \((x-x_A)^2-(x-x_B)^2 = k\). The \(x^2\) terms cancel → vertical line \(x = c\). If k = 0, P is equidistant from A and B (perpendicular bisector of AB).

9 Constructions & Loci — Quick Reference All Exam Boards Year 11 Revision Card

Use this card to revise. Each numbered section above has steps, diagrams, and a Quick Check question.

Constructions & Loci — Quick Reference

Basic Loci

  • Fixed distance from a point → circle
  • Equidistant from A and B → ⊥ bisector of AB
  • Equidistant from two lines → angle bisector
  • Fixed distance from a segment → stadium shape

Constructions

  • ⊥ bisector: equal arcs from A and B
  • Angle bisector: arc from vertex; equal arcs from leg crossings
  • 60°: equilateral triangle (AX = AY = XY = r)
  • 90°: mark X, Y equidistant from P; arcs cross above
  • 30° = bisect 60°; 45° = bisect 90°

Special Points

  • Circumcentre: where ⊥ bisectors meet
  • Incentre: where angle bisectors meet
  • Inradius r = Area ÷ semi-perimeter

Advanced Loci (Higher)

  • Midpoint of sliding ladder (length L): quarter-circle, radius L/2
  • Point on ladder (d from foot, length L): ellipse x²/(L−d)² + y²/d² = 1
  • PA:PB = k (k ≠ 1): Apollonius circle
  • PA² − PB² = c: vertical line
  • Scale: Actual = Map × n  |  Area: Actual area = Map area × n²
