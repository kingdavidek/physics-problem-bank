1 Place Value and Powers of 10 All Exam Boards Year 9

Every digit in a number has a value that depends on where it sits. This is called place value.

Take the number 4,372. Let's break it down:

  • The 4 is in the thousands column → it's worth 4,000
  • The 3 is in the hundreds column → it's worth 300
  • The 7 is in the tens column → it's worth 70
  • The 2 is in the ones column → it's worth 2

It works the same way with decimals — the columns just carry on to the right of the decimal point, getting ten times smaller each step.

Look at 6.482:

  • The 4 is in the tenths column → it's worth \(\frac{4}{10}\)
  • The 8 is in the hundredths column → it's worth \(\frac{8}{100}\)
  • The 2 is in the thousandths column → it's worth \(\frac{2}{1000}\)

\( 6.482 = 6 + \frac{4}{10} + \frac{8}{100} + \frac{2}{1000} \)

Each step to the right is ten times smaller; each step to the left is ten times larger.

Comparing decimals: Line up the decimal points and compare column by column from left to right. For example, 0.605 is bigger than 0.56 — both have 6 tenths, but 0.605 has 0 hundredths while 0.56 has 5 hundredths. Wait — actually 0.605 = 0.605 and 0.560 = 0.560. Looking at the tenths first: 6 vs 5, so 0.605 wins straight away.

Exam tip: Always write "0" before the decimal point (e.g. 0.5, not .5). Examiners will spot the missing zero!

2 Rounding to Decimal Places and Significant Figures All Exam Boards Year 9

Rounding makes a number simpler while keeping it close to the original. The rule is easy: look at the digit just after the one you're keeping. If it's 5 or more, round up. If it's 4 or less, leave it alone (round down).

Rounding to decimal places (d.p.) — count digits after the decimal point.

Example: Round 7.386 to 2 decimal places.

  • We're keeping 2 digits after the point, so we look at: 7.386
  • The third decimal digit is 6 (≥ 5), so we round up the 8 to a 9.
  • Answer: 7.39

Rounding to significant figures (s.f.) — count from the first non-zero digit.

Example: Round 0.004872 to 2 significant figures.

  • The first significant figure is the 4 (the first non-zero digit).
  • The second significant figure is 8. The next digit is 7 (≥ 5), so we round up.
  • Answer: 0.0049

Example: Round 38,642 to 3 significant figures.

\( 38,642 \approx 38,600 \)

(The 4th significant digit is 4, so we round down — the 6 stays as 6. The remaining digits become zeros as placeholders.)

⚠️ Common mistake: If you round 4,982 to 2 significant figures, the answer is 5,000, not 50. You must keep the number roughly the same size — the zeros are just placeholders!

3 Estimation and Checking Answers All Exam Boards Year 9

Estimation means swapping each number for a round number you can work with in your head. We use it to check whether a calculator answer is sensible — or to get a rough answer quickly.

The key step is to round each number to 1 significant figure first (unless the question tells you otherwise).

Example: Estimate \(\dfrac{19.8 \times 4.9}{0.51}\)

Round each number to 1 s.f.: 19.8 → 20,   4.9 → 5,   0.51 → 0.5

\( \frac{20 \times 5}{0.5} = \frac{100}{0.5} = 200 \)

If your calculator gives something around 190–205, great — that's sensible. If it gives 19 or 2,000, you've probably made a keying error.

Another example: Estimate \( \sqrt{48.6} \times 9.7 \)

  • √48.6 ≈ √49 = 7 (because 7² = 49)
  • 9.7 ≈ 10
  • Estimate: 7 × 10 = 70

Exam tip: Always show the rounded values you used — that's where the mark usually is, not the final answer.

4 Standard Form All Exam Boards Year 10 Higher Skill

Imagine writing the distance from Earth to the Sun: 150,000,000,000 m. That's a lot of zeros! Scientists use standard form to write very big (and very small) numbers neatly.

In standard form, every number looks like this:

\( A \times 10^n \quad \text{where } 1 \leq A < 10 \text{ and } n \text{ is an integer.} \)

In plain English: the first part must be a number between 1 and 10 (not including 10), and the second part is a power of 10.

Example — large number: Write 45,000 in standard form.

  • Put the decimal point after the first digit: 4.5
  • Count how many places you moved the point: 4 places to the left
  • Answer: \(4.5 \times 10^4\)

Example — small number: Write 0.0062 in standard form.

  • Put the decimal point after the first non-zero digit: 6.2
  • Count how many places you moved the point: 3 places to the right
  • The power is negative for small numbers: \(6.2 \times 10^{-3}\)
Ordinary number Standard form Why?
45,000 \(4.5 \times 10^4\) Decimal moved 4 places left.
0.0062 \(6.2 \times 10^{-3}\) Decimal moved 3 places right.

Multiplying in standard form: Multiply the front numbers and add the powers of 10.

\( (3 \times 10^5)(4 \times 10^2) = 12 \times 10^7 = 1.2 \times 10^8 \)

(We re-wrote 12 as 1.2 × 10 so the answer stays in proper standard form.)

Dividing in standard form: Divide the front numbers and subtract the powers of 10.

\( (8 \times 10^6) \div (2 \times 10^2) = 4 \times 10^4 \)

5 Percentages: Increase, Decrease and Reverse Percentages All Exam Boards Year 10

Per cent means out of 100. So 15% means 15 out of every 100 — or 0.15 as a decimal.

The multiplier method is the slickest way to do percentage changes in one step.

  • Increase by 15% → multiply by 1.15 (you keep 100% + add 15%)
  • Decrease by 15% → multiply by 0.85 (you keep 100% − lose 15%)

Example: A jacket costs £60. It goes up by 15%. What is the new price?

\( 60 \times 1.15 = \mathbf{£69} \)

Example: A laptop is reduced by 20%. It now costs £480. What was the original price?

This is a reverse percentage — we're given the after value and want the before. Divide by the multiplier instead of multiplying:

\( \text{Original} = 480 \div 0.80 = \mathbf{£600} \)

Another example: A price after a 20% increase is £72. Find the original.

\( \text{Original} = 72 \div 1.20 = \mathbf{£60} \)

⚠️ Trap question: A price goes up by 20% then down by 20%. You do not get back to the start! Try it: 100 × 1.20 = 120, then 120 × 0.80 = 96. You end up lower than you started because the 20% decrease comes off a bigger number.

🧠 Quick Check

A coat costs £80 after a 20% discount. What was the original price?

6 Error Intervals and Bounds All Exam Boards Year 11 Higher Skill

When we measure something and round it, we don't know the exact value — it could be slightly higher or lower. The full range of possible values is called the error interval.

Example: A length is measured as 8.4 cm to the nearest 0.1 cm. What is the error interval?

  • Half of 0.1 is 0.05 — that's the "half unit".
  • Subtract half unit for the lower bound: 8.4 − 0.05 = 8.35
  • Add half unit for the upper bound: 8.4 + 0.05 = 8.45

\( 8.35 \leq x < 8.45 \)

Notice: we use ≤ at the lower bound (8.35 would still round to 8.4) but a strict < at the upper bound (8.45 itself would round to 8.5, not 8.4).

Another example: A weight is given as 120 g to the nearest 10 g. Half of 10 is 5, so:

\( 115 \leq w < 125 \)

Bounds in calculations: To get the maximum possible area, use the upper bound for both lengths. To get the minimum, use lower bounds for both. For division, maximum result = upper ÷ lower.

🧠 Quick Check

A length is given as 6.3 cm to 1 decimal place. Which error interval is correct?

7 Index Laws and Negative Powers All Exam Boards Year 10

An index (or power) tells you how many times to multiply a number by itself. For example:

\( 5^3 = 5 \times 5 \times 5 = 125 \)

That's 5 multiplied together three times — not 5 × 3 = 15. That's the number-one mistake to avoid!

Here are the five index laws you need to know, each with an example first:

What it says Example first General law
Multiplying same base → add powers \(2^3 \times 2^4 = 2^7\) \(a^m \times a^n = a^{m+n}\)
Dividing same base → subtract powers \(5^6 \div 5^2 = 5^4\) \(a^m \div a^n = a^{m-n}\)
Power of a power → multiply powers \((3^2)^4 = 3^8\) \((a^m)^n = a^{mn}\)
Anything to the power 0 = 1 \(7^0 = 1\) \(a^0 = 1\) (a ≠ 0)
Negative power → flip it (reciprocal) \(2^{-3} = \frac{1}{8}\) \(a^{-n} = \frac{1}{a^n}\)

Why does \(a^0 = 1\)? Think about it this way: \(2^3 \div 2^3 = 8 \div 8 = 1\). But using the index law: \(2^{3-3} = 2^0\). So \(2^0\) must equal 1!

⚠️ Common mistake: \(3^2\) means \(3 \times 3 = 9\), not \(3 \times 2 = 6\). Powers are repeated multiplication, not ordinary multiplication.

🧠 Quick Check

Simplify \(3^5 \div 3^2\).

8 Prime Factors, HCF and LCM All Exam Boards Year 9

A prime number has exactly two factors: 1 and itself. The first few primes are 2, 3, 5, 7, 11, 13 … Notice that 1 is not prime (it only has one factor).

Every whole number bigger than 1 can be broken down into primes — this is called writing it as a product of prime factors. We use a factor tree to do this.

Example: Write 360 as a product of prime factors.

  • 360 = 2 × 180
  • 180 = 2 × 90
  • 90 = 2 × 45
  • 45 = 3 × 15
  • 15 = 3 × 5 ← all prime now

\( 360 = 2^3 \times 3^2 \times 5 \)

Once you have the prime factor form for two numbers, finding HCF and LCM is straightforward.

Example: Find the HCF and LCM of 12 and 18.

12 = 2² × 3    18 = 2 × 3²

  • HCF — take the lowest power of each shared prime: 2¹ × 3¹ = 6
  • LCM — take the highest power of every prime: 2² × 3² = 36

A handy memory trick: HCF = Highest Common Factor (so we use the lowest powers because we want what's common — the overlap). LCM = Lowest Common Multiple (so we need all prime factors to cover both numbers).

Practice Strategy Revision

Number questions reward careful working. Always write down each step — your rounded value, your multiplier, your error interval, or your prime factor form. This makes your method clear and helps you avoid calculator mistakes.

When you revise, mix short fluency drills (e.g. "write in standard form", "round to 2 s.f.") with longer context questions. GCSE exams love testing these skills inside real-world situations: money, science notation, population statistics, or measurements. If you can do both types, you're set.

Here's a quick checklist for each topic:

  • ✅ Place value: can you identify the value of any digit — including in decimals?
  • ✅ Rounding: can you round to any number of d.p. or s.f. without confusing the two?
  • ✅ Estimation: do you round to 1 s.f. first, then calculate?
  • ✅ Standard form: is your first number always between 1 and 10?
  • ✅ Percentages: do you know the multiplier for any % increase or decrease?
  • ✅ Error intervals: do you subtract/add half a unit and use the right inequality signs?
  • ✅ Index laws: are you adding (×), subtracting (÷), or multiplying (power of power)?
  • ✅ Prime factors: can you build a factor tree and use it to find HCF and LCM?
