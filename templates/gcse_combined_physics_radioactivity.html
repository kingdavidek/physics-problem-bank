Radioactivity

Edexcel GCSE Combined Science (Higher) — Physics

Atoms, isotopes and unstable nuclei

Atoms contain protons and neutrons in the nucleus, with electrons outside the nucleus. Isotopes are atoms of the same element with the same number of protons but different numbers of neutrons.

Some nuclei are unstable and emit radiation to become more stable. This process is called radioactive decay.

Alpha, beta and gamma

  • Alpha radiation is made of helium nuclei. It is strongly ionising and has a short range in air.
  • Beta radiation is a fast-moving electron emitted from the nucleus. It is moderately ionising and travels further than alpha.
  • Gamma radiation is electromagnetic radiation. It is weakly ionising and travels the furthest.

Penetration

  • Alpha is stopped by paper or skin.
  • Beta is stopped by thin aluminium.
  • Gamma is reduced by thick lead or concrete.

Gamma is the most penetrating, while alpha is the least penetrating.

Ionisation

Ionising radiation removes electrons from atoms, creating ions. Alpha is the most ionising because it is large and interacts strongly with matter; gamma is the least ionising.

Nuclear equations

Nuclear equations must balance for both mass number and atomic number.

  • Alpha particle: 42He
  • Beta particle: 0-1e
  • Gamma: no mass and no charge

Half-life

The half-life is the time taken for the number of undecayed nuclei, or the activity, of a radioactive sample to halve.

Radioactive decay is random, but half-life for a given isotope is constant.

Irradiation and contamination

Irradiation means being exposed to radiation from a source. Contamination means unwanted radioactive material gets onto or into an object or person.

Contamination is usually more dangerous than irradiation because the source stays in contact with the body.

Background radiation

Background radiation comes from sources such as rocks, cosmic rays, medical uses and radon gas from the ground.

Uses of radiation

  • Alpha: smoke alarms
  • Beta: thickness monitoring
  • Gamma: sterilising equipment and medical tracers

Fission and fusion

Fission is the splitting of a large unstable nucleus, releasing energy and neutrons. Fusion is the joining of small nuclei, also releasing energy.

Fission is used in nuclear power stations. Fusion happens in stars, but controlled fusion is difficult on Earth.

Quick facts

  • Most ionising: alpha
  • Most penetrating: gamma
  • Half-life is the time for activity or undecayed nuclei to halve
  • Contamination involves radioactive material being transferred
