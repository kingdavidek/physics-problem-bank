1 Coordinates and Axes All Exam Boards Year 7

A coordinate is a pair of numbers that tells you exactly where a point sits on a graph. We write it as (x, y).

Example: The point (3, 5) means: go 3 across (right) from the origin, then 5 up.

  • The x-coordinate is how far across (left or right).
  • The y-coordinate is how far up or down.
  • The origin is where the axes cross: (0, 0).

Remember: along the corridor, up the stairs — x first, then y.

Points in different quadrants can have positive or negative coordinates. For example, (−2, 4) is 2 left and 4 up; (3, −1) is 3 right and 1 down.

Go across first (x), then up or down (y). The point (3, 5) is 3 right and 5 up from the origin.

2 Straight-Line Graphs All Exam Boards Year 8

Most straight-line graphs follow this rule:

y = mx + c

  • m is the gradient — how steep the line is.
  • c is the y-intercept — where the line crosses the y-axis.

Example: For y = 2x + 1, the gradient is 2 (the line goes up 2 for every 1 across) and the y-intercept is 1 (it crosses the y-axis at (0, 1)).

To find the gradient between two points, use:

\[\text{gradient}=\frac{\text{change in }y}{\text{change in }x}\]

Example: From (1, 3) to (4, 9): change in y = 6, change in x = 3, so gradient = 6 ÷ 3 = 2.

Gradient = rise ÷ run = 6 ÷ 3 = 2. The line crosses the y-axis at (0, 1).

3 Plotting Linear Graphs All Exam Boards Year 8

To draw a straight-line graph, you need at least two points — then join them with a ruler.

Example: Plot y = 2x + 1.

  1. Pick easy x-values (e.g. 0, 1, 2).
  2. Work out y for each x.
  3. Plot the points and draw a straight line through them.
  • When x = 0, y = 2(0) + 1 = 1 → point (0, 1)
  • When x = 2, y = 2(2) + 1 = 5 → point (2, 5)

Plot (0, 1) and (2, 5), then draw a straight line through both. That's your graph of y = 2x + 1.

Exam tip: A third point is a good check — if it doesn't lie on your line, recalculate one of your values.

Plot (0, 1) and (2, 5), then draw a straight line through them.

4 Gradient and Intercept All Exam Boards Year 9

In y = mx + c, the number in front of x is the gradient m.

Example: y = −3x + 4 has gradient −3 and y-intercept 4.

  • Positive gradient — line slopes up from left to right (↗).
  • Negative gradient — line slopes down from left to right (↘).
  • Parallel lines have the same gradient. If one line is y = 2x + 1, any parallel line has gradient 2 (e.g. y = 2x − 5).

This is useful when an exam asks you to find the equation of a line parallel to a given one.

Parallel lines have the same gradient. Positive slopes go up; negative slopes go down.

5 Real-Life Graphs All Exam Boards Problem Solving Year 9

Real-life graphs show things like distance, time, speed, cost or temperature. The axes tell you what is being measured.

On a distance–time graph:

  • The gradient tells you the speed (distance ÷ time).
  • A horizontal line means the object is not moving (stationary).
  • A steeper line means faster speed.

Example: A car travels 60 km in 2 hours. Average speed = 60 ÷ 2 = 30 km/h. On the graph, that's the gradient of the sloped section.

Horizontal sections usually mean no change. On a distance–time graph, a flat line means the object has stopped.

Gradient = speed. A flat line means the object is not moving.

6 Quadratic Graphs All Exam Boards Year 10 Higher Skill

A quadratic graph has an x² term. The equation looks like y = ax² + bx + c. The graph is a smooth curve called a parabola (U-shape or upside-down U).

Example: y = x² + 3x + 2

  • When x = 0, y = 2 → crosses the y-axis at (0, 2).
  • When y = 0, solve x² + 3x + 2 = 0 → (x + 1)(x + 2) = 0 → x = −1 or x = −2. These are where the curve crosses the x-axis.

Quadratic graphs can be used to estimate roots (where y = 0), turning points (the highest or lowest point), and solutions to equations.

If the x² term is positive (e.g. y = x²), the parabola opens upward ∪. If it's negative (e.g. y = −x²), it opens downward ∩.

Roots are where the curve crosses the x-axis. The y-intercept is at (0, 2).

7 Cubic and Reciprocal Graphs All Exam Boards Year 10 Higher Skill

At Higher GCSE you need to recognise a few more graph shapes — you don't always have to plot them in full.

Cubic graphs have an x³ term, e.g. y = x³ or y = x³ − x. They often have an S-like shape.

Reciprocal graphs look like y = \(\frac{1}{x}\). They have two separate curved branches and never touch the axes (we call those lines asymptotes).

Example: y = \(\frac{1}{x}\) — when x is positive, y is positive; when x is negative, y is negative. The graph never crosses x = 0 or y = 0.

Recognising the shape helps you match equations to graphs quickly in the exam.

Cubic graphs have an S-shape. Reciprocal graphs have two branches that never touch the axes.

8 Solving Equations with Graphs All Exam Boards Year 10 Higher Skill

You can use graphs to solve equations — especially when algebra is awkward. The answer is where the graphs meet (intersect).

Example: Solve x² = 2x + 3 using graphs.

  1. Draw (or sketch) y = x² and y = 2x + 3 on the same axes.
  2. Find where they cross.
  3. The x-coordinates of those crossing points are the solutions.

Algebra check: x² = 2x + 3 → x² − 2x − 3 = 0 → (x − 3)(x + 1) = 0 → x = 3 or x = −1. The graph should cross at x = 3 and x = −1.

Graphs can be used to solve equations approximately when exact algebra is hard. The solution is found where two graphs intersect, or where one graph crosses a given line.

Solutions to x² = 2x + 3 are the x‑coordinates where the curves cross: x = −1 and x = 3.
