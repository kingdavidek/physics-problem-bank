Circle Theorems

Circles hide neat angle rules. Once you spot which theorem fits, you can find missing angles quickly. We will work through the main GCSE circle theorems step by step.

AQA Edexcel OCR
▶ Practice Quiz (10 MCQs)
1 CT1 – Angle at the Centre All Exam Boards Year 10

Example: Point C sits on the circle. The angle at the centre O is 80°. The angle at the edge (at C) on the same arc is half of that: 80 ÷ 2 = 40°.

Theorem CT1: The angle at the centre is twice the angle at the circumference, as long as both look at the same arc AB.

Angle at centre = 2 × Angle at circumference  (same arc)

What to watch for:

  • Point C should be on the major arc (the longer way round the circle from A to B).
  • If C is on the minor arc, use the reflex angle at the centre instead: angle ACB = ½ × reflex angle AOB.
  • When AB is a diameter, the centre angle is 180° — that leads to CT2 (angle in a semicircle = 90°).

Quick Check

The angle at the centre on arc AB is 110°. What is the angle at the circumference on the same arc?

2 CT2 – Angle in a Semicircle All Exam Boards Year 10

Example: AB is a diameter. C is any point on the circle (not A or B). Triangle ABC has a right angle at C, so angle ACB = 90°. If angle BAC = 35°, then angle ABC = 180° − 90° − 35° = 55°.

Theorem CT2: The angle in a semicircle is always 90°.

If AB is a diameter and C is on the circle, then angle ACB = 90°.

Angle in a semicircle = 90°  (AB is diameter)

Why it works: The angle at the centre on a diameter is 180°. By CT1, 180° = 2 × angle ACB, so angle ACB = 90°. CT2 is just CT1 with a diameter.

How to use it: Spot a diameter in the diagram — you instantly get a right angle. Then use “angles in a triangle add to 180°” for the rest.

Quick Check

AB is a diameter and C lies on the circle. What is angle ACB?

Quick Test – Foundational
3 CT3 – Angles in the Same Segment All Exam Boards Year 10

Example: Chord AB fixes two points on the circle. C and D both sit on the same side of AB (same bow of the circle). If angle ACB = 52°, then angle ADB = 52° as well.

Theorem CT3: Angles on the circumference that sit on the same side of chord AB and look at the same arc are equal.

Angles in the same segment are equal

Why it works: Both angles use the same arc AB. Each is half the centre angle (CT1), so they match.

Opposite bow: If C and D are on opposite sides of chord AB, then angle ACB + angle ADB = 180° (this links to CT4 and cyclic quadrilaterals).

Quick Check

C and D are on the same side of chord AB. Angle ACB = 38°. What is angle ADB?

4 CT4 – Cyclic Quadrilateral All Exam Boards Year 10

Example: Four points A, B, C, D all lie on a circle. Angle A = 110° and angle C = 70°. Check: 110° + 70° = 180° — opposite angles in a cyclic quadrilateral always add to 180°.

Theorem CT4: In a cyclic quadrilateral (all four corners on one circle), each pair of opposite angles adds up to 180°.

Opposite angles of a cyclic quadrilateral sum to 180°

Working backwards: If opposite angles add to 180°, the four points must lie on a circle.

Exterior angle tip: An exterior angle of a cyclic quadrilateral equals the interior angle at the opposite corner.

Quick Check

In cyclic quadrilateral ABCD, angle A = 95°. What is angle C?

5 CT5 & CT6 – Tangent Theorems All Exam Boards Year 11

Example (CT5): A tangent touches the circle at T. Radius OT meets the tangent at T. The angle between them is 90° — they are perpendicular.

Theorem CT5: A tangent and the radius at the point of contact always meet at 90°.

Tangent ⊥ Radius at point of contact

Example (CT6): From point P outside the circle you draw two tangents to touch at A and B. If PA = 7 cm, then PB = 7 cm as well.

Theorem CT6: Tangents from the same outside point to a circle are the same length.

PA = PB  (tangents from external point P)

Using CT6: Because PA = PB and OA = OB (radii), shape OAPB is a kite. So:

  • Angles at A and B are each 90° (CT5)
  • Angle AOB + angle APB = 180°
  • Line PO splits the kite in half and bisects angles APB and AOB

Quick Check

Two tangents are drawn from point P to a circle, touching at A and B. PA = 9 cm. How long is PB?

6 CT7 – Alternate Segment Theorem All Exam Boards Year 11

Example: Tangent and chord meet at A on the circle. The angle between them is 30°. Point C sits in the other bow (alternate segment). Then angle ACB = 30° as well.

Theorem CT7 (Alternate Segment):

The angle between a tangent and a chord at the point of contact equals the angle in the alternate segment — the bow on the other side of the chord.

Angle TAB = Angle ACB  (C in alternate segment)

How to remember it: Chord AB splits the circle into two bows. The tangent angle matches the angle in the other bow from where you stand at A.

Why it works (short): Radius OA is perpendicular to the tangent (CT5). Triangle OAB has OA = OB. CT1 links the centre angle to both the tangent–chord angle and angle ACB.

Quick Check

At A, the angle between the tangent and chord AB is 42°. C is in the alternate segment. What is angle ACB?

7 Chord Bisector & Intersecting Chords All Exam Boards Year 11

Example: Chord PQ has length 16 cm. The perpendicular from centre O meets PQ at M. Then PM = MQ = 8 cm each.

Chord Bisector Theorem: A line from the centre perpendicular to a chord cuts the chord in half. Also, the perpendicular bisector of any chord goes through the centre.

OM ⊥ PQ  ⟹  PM = MQ

Intersecting chords (inside the circle): If chords PQ and RS cross at T inside the circle:

  • PT × TQ = RT × TS
  • Angle at T = ½ (arc PR + arc QS)

Secants from outside: If two secants from point P cut the circle at A, B and C, D:

  • PA × PB = PC × PD
  • Angle at P = ½ (far arc − near arc)

With Pythagoras: Triangle OMP is right-angled at M. So OP² = OM² + PM². If you know the radius OP and how far O is from the chord (OM), you can find half the chord PM.

Quick Check

OM is perpendicular to chord PQ at M. PQ = 24 cm. How long is PM?
