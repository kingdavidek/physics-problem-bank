Geometry and Angles

Angles show how much something turns. You will learn angle facts on lines and in shapes, parallel-line rules, polygons, circle theorems, bearings, and when shapes are similar or congruent.

1 Angle Types and Basic Rules All Exam Boards Year 8

Example: Two angles on a straight line are 65° and 115°. They add to 180° because they form a straight line.

An angle measures how far something has turned, in degrees (°). Common types:

  • Acute – less than 90°
  • Right angle – exactly 90°
  • Obtuse – between 90° and 180°
  • Straight angle – exactly 180°
  • Reflex – greater than 180° (the long way round)

Three facts you will use again and again:

  • Angles on a straight line add to 180°
  • Angles around a point add to 360°
  • Vertically opposite angles are equal (when two lines cross)

In exams: Write the reason you use, e.g. "angles on a straight line sum to 180°".

Quick Check

Two angles on a straight line are 72° and x°. Find x.

2 Angles on Parallel Lines All Exam Boards Year 9

Example: Parallel lines are cut by a transversal. One alternate angle is 58°. The matching alternate angle is also 58° (Z-shape).

When a line crosses two parallel lines, you get three useful patterns:

  • Corresponding angles (F-shape) — equal
  • Alternate angles (Z-shape) — equal
  • Co-interior angles (C-shape, allied angles) — add to 180°

Memory tips:

  • F → Corresponding (same corner position, equal)
  • Z → Alternate (between the parallels, equal)
  • C → Co-interior (same side between parallels, sum to 180°)

Watch out: Only use these rules when the diagram shows the lines are parallel (arrow marks).

Quick Check

Two parallel lines are cut by a transversal. Co-interior angles are 110° and x°. Find x.

3 Triangles All Exam Boards Year 9

Example: A triangle has angles 50° and 60°. The third angle is 180° − 50° − 60° = 70°.

Key triangle angle facts:

  • Angles in any triangle add to 180°
  • Equilateral triangle: all angles are 60°
  • Isosceles triangle: two equal sides → two equal base angles
  • Exterior angle = sum of the two interior angles not next to it

The dashed line through C is parallel to AB. Alternate angles show the angle at C matches the angle at A (and similarly for b). Those three angles lie on a straight line, so they add to 180°.

4 Polygon Angles All Exam Boards Year 10

Example: A pentagon has 5 sides. Split it into 3 triangles from one corner — each triangle is 180°, so interior angles add to 3 × 180° = 540°. Each angle in a regular pentagon is 540° ÷ 5 = 108°.

For any polygon with n sides, we use these rules:

\(\text{Sum of interior angles} = (n-2)\times180°\)

\(\text{Each interior angle (regular)} = \dfrac{(n-2)\times180°}{n}\)

\(\text{Each exterior angle (regular)} = \dfrac{360°}{n}\)

At any corner, interior angle + exterior angle = 180° (they lie on a straight line).

5 Circle Theorems Higher Skill Year 11

Example: AB is a diameter of a circle. C is on the circle. Angle ACB = 90° (angle in a semicircle).

On GCSE Higher you need seven circle theorems. Learn them with a sketch and name the theorem when you use it:

  1. Angle at the centre = 2 × angle at the circumference (same arc)
  2. Angle in a semicircle = 90° (angle on a diameter)
  3. Angles in the same segment are equal
  4. Opposite angles in a cyclic quadrilateral add to 180°
  5. Tangent ⊥ radius at the point of contact
  6. Tangents from one outside point are equal in length
  7. Alternate segment: angle between tangent and chord = angle in the alternate segment

Another example: A tangent at T makes a 65° angle with chord TA. The angle in the alternate segment (at B on the major arc) is also 65°.

In exams: You often chain two or three theorems. Label each angle and write which rule you used.

6 Bearings Problem Solving Year 11

Example: Town B is on a bearing of 065° from A. You turn clockwise from North at A until you face B — that is 65°. We write it as 065° (three digits).

A bearing is an angle measured clockwise from North. Always use three digits: 047°, 128°, 270°.

Back-bearing: From B back to A, add 180° if the first bearing is under 180°; subtract 180° if it is 180° or more. So 065° from A → 245° from B.

Watch out: Measure from the North line at the place you start from, not from where you are going to.

Quick Check

The bearing of B from A is 047°. What is the back-bearing of A from B?

7 Similar and Congruent Shapes Higher Skill Year 10

Example: Two similar triangles have scale factor k = 2 (lengths double). A small triangle has area 4 cm². The large area = 4 × \(2^2\) = 4 × 4 = 16 cm².

Congruent means same shape and same size (you could place one on top of the other). Similar means same shape, different size — matching angles equal, sides in proportion.

Two triangles are congruent if any of these match:

  • SSS – all three sides equal
  • SAS – two sides and the angle between them equal
  • ASA – two angles and the side between them equal
  • RHS – right angle, hypotenuse, and one other side equal

For similar shapes, matching angles are equal. If lengths are multiplied by k:

\(\text{Area is multiplied by } k^2 \qquad \text{Volume is multiplied by } k^3\)

Exam tip: Match up corresponding sides first, then work out the scale factor.

Quick Check

Similar triangles have scale factor 3. The smaller has area 5 cm². What is the larger area?

Geometry & Angles — Quick Reference

Lines & points

  • Straight line = 180°
  • Around a point = 360°
  • Vertically opposite angles are equal

Parallel lines

  • Corresponding (F) = equal
  • Alternate (Z) = equal
  • Co-interior (C) = 180°

Triangles

  • Angles sum to 180°
  • Exterior = sum of two interior opposites

Polygons

  • Interior sum = \((n-2) \times 180°\)
  • Regular interior = \((n-2) \times 180 / n\)
  • Exterior angle = \(360° / n\)

Circle theorems

  • Centre angle = 2 × circumference angle
  • Angle in semicircle = 90°
  • Same segment → equal angles
  • Cyclic quad: opposite angles = 180°
  • Tangent ⊥ radius; tangents from a point are equal
