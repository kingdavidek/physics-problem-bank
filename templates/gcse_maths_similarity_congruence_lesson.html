Similarity and Congruence

Congruent shapes match exactly — same size and shape, like two identical puzzle pieces. Similar shapes look the same but one is a scaled-up or scaled-down copy. We use clear rules to prove congruence or similarity in GCSE questions.

1 Congruence — The Four Conditions All Exam Boards Year 10

Example: Triangle 1 has sides 5 cm, 7 cm, 9 cm. Triangle 2 has the same three sides in the same order. The triangles fit exactly — they are congruent by SSS.

Two triangles are congruent (≅) if any one of these four conditions is true:

SSS

Side-Side-Side
All 3 sides equal

SAS

Side-Angle-Side
2 sides + included angle

ASA

Angle-Side-Angle
2 angles + included side

RHS

Right angle–Hyp–Side
Right triangles only

AAA does NOT prove congruence — only similarity (same angles, maybe different size).
SSA is not enough — two sides plus a non-included angle can make two different triangles.

Quick Check

Two triangles have all three pairs of sides equal. Which condition proves they are congruent?

2 Similar Shapes — Scale Factor and Ratio All Exam Boards Year 10

Example: A small triangle has a side of 6 cm. The matching side on a similar large triangle is 9 cm. The linear scale factor is 9 ÷ 6 = 1.5 (or 3/2).

Two shapes are similar (~) when matching angles are equal and matching sides are in the same ratio. That ratio is the linear scale factor (LSF).

What you need to remember:

  • Matching angles are equal.
  • Matching sides are in the same ratio (the LSF).
  • LSF = (bigger corresponding side) ÷ (smaller corresponding side).
  • Missing side? Multiply the known corresponding side by the LSF.
  • Perimeters scale by the LSF too.

Quick Check

Similar triangles: small side 8 cm, matching large side 12 cm. What is the LSF (small → large)?

3 Similar Triangles — AA Condition All Exam Boards Year 10

Example: In two triangles, angle A = angle P = 50° and angle B = angle Q = 70°. The third angles must both be 180° − 50° − 70° = 60°. The triangles are similar (AA).

For triangles, you only need two matching angles (AA) to prove similarity. The third angle is fixed because angles in a triangle add to 180°.

Steps we use in exams:

  1. Find two pairs of equal angles.
  2. Say why they are equal (shared angle, parallel lines, vertically opposite, etc.).
  3. Write: “By AA similarity, triangle … ~ triangle …”
  4. Work out the scale factor from a known pair of sides.
  5. Multiply or divide to find any missing side.

Worked example: In triangle ABC, DE ∥ BC, AD = 4, DB = 8, DE = 5.
Find BC.
AB = 4 + 8 = 12. LSF = AB/AD = 12/4 = 3.
BC = DE × 3 = 5 × 3 = 15.

Quick Check

DE is parallel to BC, so △ADE ~ △ABC. AD = 3, AB = 9, DE = 4. What is BC?

5 Parallel Lines and Overlapping Similar Triangles All Exam Boards Year 11

Example: DE is parallel to BC inside triangle ABC. AD = 2 and DB = 4, so AB = 6. The scale factor from small triangle ADE to big triangle ABC is 6 ÷ 2 = 3.

These two set-ups appear a lot in GCSE papers:

Case 1: DE ∥ BC within triangle ABC

Angle A is common; angle ADE = angle ABC (corresponding).
SF = AD/AB = AE/AC = DE/BC

Case 2: AB ∥ DE — "bowtie" configuration

Lines AD and BE cross at O. Vertically opposite angles at O are equal; alternate angles because AB ∥ DE.
△AOB ~ △DOE (by AA)

Basic Proportionality Theorem (BPT): If DE ∥ BC, then

AD/DB = AE/EC     and     AD/AB = AE/AC = DE/BC

Quick Check

DE ∥ BC. AD = 5, DB = 10. What is AB?

6 Writing Formal Proofs All Exam Boards Year 11

Example: You spot angle A = angle P and angle B = angle Q. You write both facts with reasons, then finish with “∴ △ABC ~ △PQR (AA similarity).” That is enough for full similarity marks.

Proof template for similarity (AA):

In triangles ABC and PQR:
1. Angle A = Angle P [reason — e.g. given / corresponding / alternate / vertically opposite]
2. Angle B = Angle Q [reason]
∴ Triangle ABC ~ Triangle PQR  (AA similarity)

Proof template for congruence (SSS example):

In triangles ABM and ACM:
1. AB = AC  [isosceles triangle]
2. BM = MC  [M is midpoint of BC]
3. AM = AM  [common side]
∴ Triangle ABM ≅ Triangle ACM  (SSS)

Exam tip: Give a reason for every line. End with the condition: “SSS congruence” or “AA similarity”. Do not skip the final statement.

Quick Check

You have proved two pairs of equal angles in two triangles. What should you write at the end?

7 Real-World Applications All Exam Boards Year 10

Example (shadows): A 1.8 m person casts a 2.4 m shadow. A tree casts a 10 m shadow. Since the ratio of height and shadow for the tree will be very close to that of the person, we can set up a proportion and solve for the height of the tree: h = 1.8 × 10 ÷ 2.4 = 7.5 m.

Map scales: A map scale of 1 : n means 1 cm on the map = n cm in real life.

  • Distances scale by n: actual distance = map distance × n (then convert units).
  • Areas scale by n²: actual area = map area × n².

Scale models: A scale of 1 : n applied to a 3-D model:

  • Lengths: actual = model × n
  • Areas (and surface areas): actual = model area × n²
  • Volumes: actual = model volume × n³

Shadow problems: At the same time of day, a person and a tree cast shadows. The triangles formed by person/shadow and tree/shadow are similar.

Same shadow question again: 1.8/2.4 = h/10 → h = 7.5 m. The person-and-shadow triangle is similar to the tree-and-shadow triangle.

Quick Check

A 1.6 m lamp post casts a 2 m shadow. A building casts a 15 m shadow at the same time. How tall is the building?
